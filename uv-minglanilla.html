10. X-raysUV raysOrganismUV raysEnvironmentGamma rays​


X-ray

X-rays are electromagnetic radiation that can see through a person's skin and provide images of the bones beneath it. Advances in technology have resulted in more powerful and focussed X-ray beams, as well as a growing number of applications for these light waves, ranging from viewing teeny biological cells to eliminating cancer cells.

Soft X-rays and hard X-rays are the two types of X-rays. Soft X-rays have comparatively small wavelengths of roughly 10 nanometers (a nanometer is one billionth of a meter), and hence fall between ultraviolet (UV) light and gamma-rays in the electromagnetic (EM) spectrum. The wavelengths of hard X-rays are around 100 picometers (a picometer is one-trillionth of a meter).

14. what property is this If UV=KL and KL=6,then UV=6​


Answer:

The situation is described as the transitive property of Equality

Step-by-step explanation:

Transitive property states that if one value is equal to the second value, and the second value is equal to the third value, then the first value should be equal to the third value.

Different Properties of Equality Numbers

1. Commutative Property

2. Distributive Property

3. Associative Property

4. Reflexive Property

5. Symmetric Property

6. Transitive Property

In notation form, Transitive property is  "if [tex]a=b[/tex] and [tex]b=c[/tex], then [tex]a=c[/tex]".

So the situation "If [tex]UV=KL[/tex] and [tex]KL=6[/tex],then [tex]UV=6[/tex]" is justified by the Transitive property of Equality.

22. X-rays UV rays Organism Environment Gamma rays UV rays​


Answer: There are two kinds of radiation: non-ionizing radiation and ionizing radiation. Non-ionizing radiation has enough energy to move atoms in a molecule around or cause them to vibrate, but not enough to remove electrons from atoms. Examples of this kind of radiation are radio waves, visible light, and microwaves. Ionizing radiation has so much energy it can knock electrons out of atoms, a process known as ionization.
