6. ​​​​​​​A transformer is a device which step-up or step-down the level of AC current or voltage. Can a transformer be used in DC circuits? Why or why not? Differentiate AC from a DC circuit.


The basic principle of a transformer actually depends upon induction. So it is clear that when we apply a voltage across the primary coil of the transformer, it magnetizes the core of it and due to this magnetic field, there comes an EMF across the secondary coil following the Faraday’s Law. Now, if we apply the DC voltage across the primary, the magnitude of voltage is uniform with respect to time. So there will be no magnetic field in the core and the transformer will not work. But due to constant voltage supply to the iron core, the core will turn into a electromagnetic rather producing a magnetic field across it. Thus, the answer is no. You cannot use DC cicrcuits in transformers. Why not? As transformers are based on mutual inductance property, DC doesn’t have this property because of non varying flux produced by it. Therefore no EMF would be generated in the secondary winding of transformer. Hence, no transformation of power from primary winding to secondary winding.

The difference between AC and DC lies in the direction in which the electrons flow. In DC, the electrons flow steadily in a single direction, or "forward." In AC, electrons keep switching directions, sometimes going "forward" and then going "backward." It describes the flow of charge that changes direction periodically. As a result, the voltage level also reverses along with the current.

Most digital multimeters have a reverse polarity display, but most analog meters do not. Reversing the polarity on an analog meter could possibly bend the needle and damage the meter movement. If your power source uses different colors, CHECK THE MANUAL for the correct polarity of each conductor.

Most circuits have a mixture of DC (direct current) and AC (alternating current) parts. The DC portion provides power for the active devices whereas the AC is normally signal information (eg voice audio, digital data). So checking the health of a circuit is normally necessary because the signal side has stopped or been corrupted by the DC part.In DC circuits, polarity of voltages and currents can be good indicators of where faults lie. Component failures can cause power supply problems, and overheating. 19. A time of 10 milliseconds is required for the current on a series RL dc circuit to reach 90% of its final steady-state value. What is the time constant in seconds for the circuit? Answer: 4.34 ms Show solution.


Answer:

The time constant (represented by the symbol τ) of an RL circuit is defined as the product of the resistance (R) and the inductance (L) of the circuit, i.e., τ = L/R.

In this problem, we are given that it takes 10 milliseconds for the current on a series RL dc circuit to reach 90% of its final steady-state value. We can use this information to determine the time constant of the circuit.

Let's use the following formula to calculate the value of time constant:

t = τ * ln(1/0.1)

where t is the time required for the current to reach 90% of its final steady-state value, ln is the natural logarithm function, and 1/0.1 is the ratio of the final steady-state value of the current to the value of the current at time t.

Substituting the given values, we get:

10 ms = τ * ln(1/0.1)

Rearranging this equation to solve for τ, we have:

τ = 10 ms / ln(1/0.1)

Using a calculator to evaluate the natural logarithm and perform the division, we get:

τ ≈ 4.34 ms

Therefore, the time constant of the RL circuit is approximately 4.34 milliseconds.

28. why won't a transformer work in a dc circuit​


Why won't a transformer work in a dc circuit ?

- Direct current (DC) has no time varying field because current is constant as well as there is no relatively motion between coil and core(magnetic circuit) of the transformer. So there is no induced emf in secondary coil of the transformer. Thus DC supply is not used for transformer.
