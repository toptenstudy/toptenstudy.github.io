2. The reciprocal ofcalor is​


Answer:

In thermodynamics, heat is energy in transfer to or from a thermodynamic system, by mechanisms other than thermodynamic work or transfer of matter.[1][2][3][4][5][6][7] The various mechanisms of energy transfer that define heat are stated in the next section of this article.

The Sun and Earth form an ongoing example of a heating process. Some of the Sun's thermal radiation strikes and heats the Earth. Compared to the Sun, Earth has a much lower temperature and so sends far less thermal radiation back to the Sun. The heat of this process can be quantified by the net amount, and direction (Sun to Earth), of energy it transferred in a given period of time.

Like thermodynamic work, heat transfer is a process involving more than one system, not a property of any one system. In thermodynamics, energy transferred as heat contributes to change in the system's cardinal energy variable of state, for example its internal energy, or for example its enthalpy. This is to be distinguished from the ordinary language conception of heat as a property of an isolated system.

The quantity of energy transferred as heat in a process is the amount of transferred energy excluding any thermodynamic work that was done and any energy contained in matter transferred. For the precise definition of heat, it is necessary that it occur by a path that does not include transfer of matter.[8]

Though not immediately by the definition, but in special kinds of process, quantity of energy transferred as heat can be measured by its effect on the states of interacting bodies. For example, respectively in special circumstances, heat transfer can be measured by the amount of ice melted, or by change in temperature of a body in the surroundings of the system.[9] Such methods are called calorimetry.

The conventional symbol used to represent the amount of heat transferred in a thermodynamic process is Q. As an amount of energy (being transferred), the SI unit of heat is the joule (J).


3. explain caloric theory of heat​


Answer:

The caloric theory is an obsolete scientific theory that heat consists of a self-repellent fluid called caloric that flows from hotter bodies to colder bodies. Caloric was also thought of as a weightless gas that could pass in and out of pores in solids and liquids. The "caloric theory" was superseded by the mid-19th century in favor of the mechanical theory of heat, but nevertheless persisted in some scientific literature—particularly in more popular treatments—until the end of the 19th century.

22. Type TRUE if the given statement is correct and FALSE if it is incorrect. You must net at or below your BMR in terms of caloric intake.


Type TRUE if the given statement is correct and FALSE if it is incorrect. You must net at or below your BMR in terms of caloric intake.

The answer is False. Here are the explanation.

Caloric intake is also modestly influenced by environmental temperature: the  intake of energy rises at high and low ambient temperatures. Power consumption also influences  the intake of calories. Long-distance runners, for example, have high calorie intakes.  More moderate levels of physical activity can increase the intake of food by lean, but not  by obese subjects. Woo et al. (1985) compared the impact of three rates of physical activity  on food consumption by normal-weight and obese subjects in controlled metabolic ward studies. They observed that lean women increased their food consumption in proportion to the rise  in energy expenditure as energy expends decreased by increasing the time spent on physical activity.  On the other hand, obese women did not (Woo et al., 1985). A new life with own power stores is created during pregnancy. This means that pregnant women are  usually recommended to increase their consumption of food (NRC, 1980). According to recent studies in several countries, energy demands and food intakes may be less than previously indicated during  pregnancy (Durnin, 1986). (See the energy balance in special circumstances section on parenting,  below.)

To really get how much time you need to exercise, you first need to look at your basal metabolic rate, or BMR. This is the amount of energy (calories) that you burn at rest and that your body uses to survive.  The basic rule of thumb is that, in terms of caloric intake, you must absolutely net at or above your BMR.  This means that when you subtract the amount of exercise calories you burned from your total calories  for a day, the difference is at least what your body needs to support fundamental physiological processes.

Why BMR Matters When you're trying to exercise and eat right to lose weight, netting at least your BMR is non-negotiable  because your body will eventually think you're going to starve if your caloric intake doesn't  meet the energy requirements. As the body tries to figure out how to deal with perceived starvation,  stored carbohydrates, fats or proteins can be used as energy sources. Protein is what makes the muscle mass lean. Normally, the body uses muscle metabolism as a last resort, but lean muscle tissue requires more calories than other tissues.

The body will metabolize muscle as a way to reduce the amount of calories you need per day.  That's hardly what anyone looking to get ripped off wants, and it can be especially dangerous  considering that one of the most vital organs in the body — the heart — is a muscle. In fact, the heart  can decrease in size, slow down, and eventually fail. As your metabolism slows down and you lose your  lean muscle, it's getting harder and harder to eat "normal" amounts of calories without gaining weight,  simply because you've trained your body to do less and hang on to anything extra.

26. how does caloric balance work?​


Answer:

Calorie balance means that the calories we take in through eating and drinking should equal the calories we use through physical activity and our body's other energy needs (for example, breathing, digesting food, sleeping). A rule of thumb is that one mile of brisk walking uses about 100 calories.
