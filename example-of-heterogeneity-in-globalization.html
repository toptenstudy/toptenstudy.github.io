6. example of heterogeneous​


Answer:

A heterogeneous mixture is simply any mixture that is not uniform in composition - it's a non-uniform mixture of smaller constituent parts. By contrast, a mixture that is uniform in composition is a homogeneous mixture. For the purposes of this discussion, “not uniform” means anything that clearly has different parts visible to the naked eye. You can find many examples of heterogeneous mixtures in solid, liquid and gaseous form throughout nature.

Example:

Concrete is a heterogeneous mixture of an aggregate: cement, and water.

Sugar and sand form a heterogeneous mixture.

Ice cubes in cola form a heterogeneous mixture.

Salt and pepper form a heterogeneous mixture.

Chocolate chip cookies are a heterogeneous mixture.

8. Compare and contrast homogeneity and heterogeneity theory in globalization?


Answer:

Homogeneity theory in globalization suggests that as the world becomes more interconnected and globalized, cultural and societal differences will become less pronounced and we will see a convergence of cultures, values, and norms. This theory posits that the forces of globalization will lead to a homogenization of cultures and a loss of diversity.

On the other hand, heterogeneity theory in globalization suggests that as the world becomes more interconnected and globalized, cultural and societal differences will become more pronounced and we will see a divergence of cultures, values, and norms. This theory posits that the forces of globalization will lead to a diversification of cultures and an increase in diversity.

In summary, homogeneity theory in globalization suggests that globalization will lead to a decrease in cultural diversity, while heterogeneity theory suggests that globalization will lead to an increase in cultural diversity.

Answer:

Homogeneity and heterogeneity are two theories that attempt to explain the effects of globalization on cultural identity and diversity.

Homogeneity theory posits that globalization leads to the homogenization of cultures, where the spread of global capitalism and Westernization results in the erosion of traditional cultures and the emergence of a single, dominant culture. According to this theory, as countries become more interconnected through trade and communication, they will begin to adopt similar values, customs, and practices, leading to a loss of cultural diversity.

On the other hand, Heterogeneity theory argues that globalization leads to the diversification of cultures, where the exchange of ideas and information between different societies results in the emergence of a more diverse and complex global culture. According to this theory, as countries become more interconnected, they will be exposed to a wider range of cultural influences, leading to a greater diversity of customs, practices, and values.

One important thing to note is that both theories can be true to some extent, depending on the context and how globalization is happening. In some cases, globalization can lead to the erosion of traditional cultures, but in others, it can lead to the emergence of new cultural forms. Also, one should note that both theories assume that cultural identity is something that is fixed and unchanging, but in reality, cultural identity is a dynamic and complex concept that is shaped by multiple factors.

21. how can you deferenciate homogeneous and heterogeneous in globalization​


Answer:

The terms homogeneity and heterogeneity are used to describe the uniformity and regularity in spatial distribution of geomaterial properties in natural subsurface systems. Homogeneity means spatially uniform-distributed properties. In natural systems, geological media are almost always heterogeneous. That is, their physical and (bio)geochemical properties vary spatially. Spatial heterogeneity can refer to both physical and geochemical properties. Physical properties include mineral grain size, porosity, and permeability / conductivity. Geochemical properties include, for example, mineral types, lithology, mineral surface area, and cation exchange capacity.
