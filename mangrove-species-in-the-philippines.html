Philippine coasts have a powerful natural defense: their mangroves. Many coastal habitats, including mangroves, coral reefs and salt marshes, can significantly reduce flooding and erosion, and thus protect people and property from the damages caused by storms, sea level rise and king tides.

MANGROVE SWAMPS

Mangrove forests are productive wetlands found in coastal intertidal zones. They are also known as mangrove swamps, mangrove thickets, or mangals. Because mangrove trees, they only grow in tropical and subtropical climates. Coastal wetlands prevalent in tropical and subtropical climates are known as mangrove swamps. Halophytic (salt-loving) trees, shrubs, and other plants grow in brackish to saltwater tidal waters, giving them their name. Mangroves play a vital role in the ecology as well. Their deep roots aid in the binding and construction of soils. Birds, fish, invertebrates, animals, and plants all use mangrove forests for habitat and refuge. Because of its mangrove forests, the Siargao Islands are the largest protected area under the National Integrated Protected Area System Act. Del Carmen, the Philippines' largest mangrove, has 4,000 hectares of contiguous mangrove.
