7. What is the connection between literacy and culture


Literacy in the simplest definition is the ability to read and write. We can recognize, understand, interpret, create, communicate and compute, using printed and written materials associated with varying contexts. Culture, on the other hand, can be defined as the customary beliefs, social forms, and material traits of a racial, religious, or social group. Literature comes from culture and life experiences. Literacy gives us the ability to appreciate literature made based on the different cultures in the world. With that, cultural literacy was introduced. Cultural literacy referred to as the ability to understand and participate in a given or specific culture.

Characteristics of Culture

Here are the five basic characteristics that all cultures share:

Culture is learned because it’s not biologically inherited.Culture is shared with other members of our group. Culture is interconnected.Culture is dynamic as we interact with each other. Symbols are an integral part of every culture and it varies across different cultures.

Ways to support literacy development

There are numerous everyday things you can do to promote literacy learning. These include:

Sharing your knowledge and teaching how you use literacy in your everyday lifeEncouraging your child to read and writePlaying games that develop learning and enjoyment of wordsDiscussing new and unfamiliar words or phrases and exploring these through print and electronic dictionaries

Answer:

The political and cultural landscape of a society can have a significant impact on the development of science culture, science activities, and science literacy. Political factors such as government policies, funding, and regulations can shape the direction and priorities of scientific research. Cultural factors such as attitudes towards science and technology, religion, language, and ethnicity can also influence the public's engagement with science-related activities and their level of science literacy. Overall, these factors are crucial in shaping the public's understanding and appreciation of science in a society.
