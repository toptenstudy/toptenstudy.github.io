1. What are the effects of suicidal thoughts?


And while many attempted suicides are impulsive acts during a moment of crisis, they can leave you with permanent serious or severe injuries, such as organ failure or brain damage. For those left behind after a suicide — people known as survivors of suicide — grief, anger, depression and guilt are common.

Suicidal thoughts have many causes. Most often, suicidal thoughts are the result of feeling like you can't cope when you're faced with what seems to be an overwhelming life situation. If you don't have hope for the future, you may mistakenly think suicide is a solution. You may experience a sort of tunnel vision, where in the middle of a crisis you believe suicide is the only way out.

There also may be a genetic link to suicide. People who complete suicide or who have suicidal thoughts or behavior are more likely to have a family history of suicide.

Detecting the immediate risk of suicide is a very difficult, but potentially lifesaving endeavor. Present standard predictors do not adequately address the issue of acute or immediate suicide risk, but seem to be more correlated with longer term risk. Although the presence of a suicide plan suggests acute risk for suicide, it is clear that many patients deny thoughts or plans that are at immediate risk for suicide, and information from significant others should be sought and taken seriously despite patient denials. A careful delineation of past suicidal tendencies may be extrapolated to a current episode in patients with a past history of suicidality. Recent findings suggest that agitation, severe psychic anxiety, panic attacks, severe global insomnia, severe anhedonia, and recent-onset alcohol abuse may also help classify a depressed patient in an acute suicide risk group. Bipolar patients may manifest agitation and severe psychic anxiety as well as severe insomnia in mixed dysphoric states during the course of their illness. It is important that these symptoms be recognized and aggressively treated with long-acting benzodiazepines or atypical antipsychotic medications to reduce acute risk in whatever setting treatment can take place. The use of maintenance lithium carbonate or even clozapine, and possibly other atypical antipsychotic medications, may be useful in the management of some patients with high chronic suicide risk features. Evidence shows that although short-term antidepressant treatment (8 weeks) does not seem to reduce suicide risk (although it has been shown to reduce suicidal ideation), treatment with antidepressants, antipsychotic medications, and lithium (in various combinations) for a minimum of 6 months has been shown to reduce suicide by 2.5 times in a chronic high-risk group with both bipolar and unipolar disorders. Certain forms of psychotherapy, particularly Dialectic Behavioral Therapy, and perhaps Cognitive Behavioral and Interpersonal Therapy, may reduce long-term suicide risk.


15. what to do to get rid of perfectionism, suicidal thoughts, ADHD & social anxiety? ​

Dealing with perfectionism, suicidal thoughts, ADHD, and social anxiety can be a challenging and complex process. It is important to seek professional help from a licensed therapist or psychiatrist to develop a personalized treatment plan that suits your individual needs. However, here are some general tips that may help:

Practice self-compassion: Instead of being hard on yourself, practice self-kindness and remind yourself that making mistakes is a part of the learning process.

Set realistic goals: Break larger tasks into smaller, manageable ones and set realistic deadlines. Celebrate your achievements, no matter how small they may seem.

Seek social support: Reach out to friends, family members, or support groups for help and encouragement. Don't hesitate to ask for assistance when you need it.

Seek professional help: Consider seeing a licensed therapist or psychiatrist who can provide you with specialized treatment and support.

Remember that recovery is a journey, and it takes time and effort. Be patient and kind to yourself, and don't hesitate to reach out for help

Stress and Stress Management

Stress defined

Stress is a term widely used in the aspects of psychology and physical health. Throughout the years, stress has affected people in various ways, most notably by affecting them directly. Thus, it is of utmost importance to have a proper knowledge and understanding regarding stress so people would be aware of the early warning signs.

Stress is an emotional feeling. It causes different reactions from an individual which range from being frustrated, being sad, being emotional to being depressed. It is the product of the things we experienced, as well as the feedbacks we get from other people.

Causes of stress

Stress is caused by a lot of things. These range from very minute and little things to the most intense ones. There are those who get stressed by simple things, whereas there are those, on the other hand, that only get stressed when they are put under extreme pressure or are experiencing really challenging experiences. Below are some of the factors that cause stress:

Pressure (from yourself, parents, and friends)Expectations (from yourself, parents, and friends)Noise (most especially, loud noises)Unhealthy dietLack of things (money, etc)Death of a loved oneMoving from one place to anotherDivorceTerminal illness or diseaseAnxiety and low self esteem

Stages of stress

Since stress is a valid emotional feeling, individuals who experience stress undergo various stages. The three most known stages of stress are the following:

Alarm stageResistance stageExhaustion stage

Alarm stage

The alarm stage is the initial stage wherein the symptoms of stress are most notable. Some individuals have the tendency to react against the event. This includes getting out of the place as soon as possible or shutting people up to the stress will not be triggered. It affects us by increasing our heartbeats or giving us uneasy feelings.

Resistance stage

This is the second stage of stress. This is where our bodies begin to calm down and lower our heart beats. Aside from that, there are also signs that can be found on this stage such as irritation, frustration, etc.

Exhaustion stage

This is the last stage of stress and the most dangerous one. We should not let stress get this far. When we experiences stress for long periods of time, it affects our overall physical, mental, and emotional health. Signs of this include burnout, depression, etc.

Stress Management

There are hundreds of ways to manage stress. Some of the ways of managing stress are mentioned below:

Think positivelyAvoid overthinkingTalk with someoneSleep wellTake medicinesSeek a help from psychiatristDo something that you enjoyTravel

Most people can be helped in getting through their moment of crisis if they have someone who will spend time with them, listen, take them seriously and help them talk about their thoughts and feelings. Almost every suicidal crisis has at its center a strong ambivalence: "I can't handle the pain anymore," but not necessarily, "I want to be dead forever!" What most suicidal people want is not to be dead but some way to get through the terrible pain they are experiencing and someone they can turn to during those terrible moments of fear and desperation.

Having someone to talk to can make a big difference. However, you may need to be persistent before they are willing to talk. Talking about suicide or suicidal thoughts will not push someone to kill themselves. It is also not true that people who talk about killing themselves will not actually try it. Take any expressed intention of suicide very seriously. While you may not be able to solve these problems for a friend or classmate, you may be able to help the person find someone who can help.
