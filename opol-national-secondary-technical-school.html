In general, information sources are divided into 3, namely primary, secondary and tertiary sources of information. Explanations and examples of each can be seen below:

1. Primary Information Source

Is a source of information that is original and written in full. Usually a kind of book that is in the library, but it could also be in other sources. The status of an information center can dominantly determine the types of information it manages.

For example, the university library develops more information in the form of textbooks and journals, while the public library presents more general science books as well. Examples of primary information sources are as follows:

Monographs, in the form of textbooks by a single author, multiple authors, editors, and translations.Magazine articles, usually in the form of research results and equipped with abstracts and illustrations made by the author.Research results, are the results of new research or discovery based on a hypothesis and examined for its truth. This book can also be a continuation or development of previous research.Direct reports or reports are the results of interviews with someone, direct reports, theses, theses, and others - others.

2. Secondary Information Source

The definition of secondary information sources is a collection of information in the form of bibliographic data that is quoted from primary information sources and is used to facilitate retrieval and retrieval of primary information which is usually in the form of magazines or books. Examples of secondary sources of information are encyclopedias, dictionaries, bibliographies, index collections, abstracts, biographical sources, and catalogs in libraries.

3. Tertiary Information Source

If the secondary information source is a collection of information that facilitates the search for secondary information, then the tertiary information source is a summary of the secondary information source. An example is an abstract index which is a collection of abstracts and makes it easier for us to do abstract searches. Another example is in bibliographic lists published in magazine form.
