Explanation of the evolution of the computer generation

1st Generation

This was from the period of 1940 to 1955. This was when machine language was developed for the use of computers. They used vacuum tubes for the circuitry. For the purpose of memory, they used magnetic drums.

2nd Generation

Here they advanced from vacuum tubes to transistors. This made the computers smaller, faster and more energy efficient. And they advanced from binary to assembly languages.

3rd Generation

The hallmark of this period (1964-1971) was the development of the integrated circuit.

4th Generation

The invention of the microprocessors brought along the fourth generation of computers. This was when we started producing computers for home use. Another important aspect is the development of higher computer languages like C++ and Java.

5th Generation

This is the present and the future of the computer world. The defining aspect of this generation is artificial intelligence. The use of parallel processing and superconductors are making this a reality and provide a lot of scope for the future.

28. Explain the types of evolution of computer and explain the pros and cons of the evolution until present.


Answer:

The evolution of computers can be broadly divided into five generations, each of which represents a significant advancement in hardware and software technology. Here are brief explanations of each generation and their pros and cons until the present:

1. First Generation (1940s-1950s): The first generation of computers used vacuum tubes for circuitry and were very large and expensive. They were primarily used for scientific and military applications.

Pros:

- They were the first electronic computers, marking a significant advancement over mechanical computers.

- They could perform complex calculations faster than earlier machines.

Cons:

- They were very large and expensive, limiting their accessibility to a few organizations.

- They were prone to overheating and required frequent maintenance.

2. Second Generation (1950s-1960s): The second generation of computers used transistors for circuitry, which were smaller and more reliable than vacuum tubes. They were also more powerful and efficient.

Pros:

- They were smaller and more reliable than first-generation computers.

- They were faster and more powerful than their predecessors, making them suitable for a wider range of applications.

Cons:

- They were still very expensive, limiting their accessibility to large organizations.

- They generated a lot of heat, requiring specialized cooling systems.

3. Third Generation (1960s-1970s): The third generation of computers used integrated circuits, which allowed for even greater miniaturization and efficiency. This led to the development of the first commercially available microprocessors.

Pros:

- They were smaller and more powerful than previous generations, making them suitable for a wider range of applications.

- They were less expensive than earlier computers, making them more accessible to smaller organizations.

Cons:

- They still required specialized cooling systems to prevent overheating.

- They were still relatively expensive compared to today's computers.

4. Fourth Generation (1970s-1990s): The fourth generation of computers saw the development of personal computers and the widespread use of graphical user interfaces (GUIs). This made computers more user-friendly and accessible to the general public.

Pros:

- They were much smaller and more affordable than previous generations, making them accessible to individuals and small businesses.

- They were more user-friendly, thanks to the development of GUIs.

Cons:

- They were still relatively expensive compared to today's computers.

- They were not as powerful as later generations, limiting their capabilities for certain applications.
