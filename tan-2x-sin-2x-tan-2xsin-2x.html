[tex] \begin{array}{l} \large \bold{Implicit\ Differentiation} \\ \\ \bold{Given:}\: \sf 4 \ln (\sec y + \tan y) - 2x - \sin 2x = 0 \\ \\ \textsf{Take the derivative of every variable, but when} \\ \textsf{we take the derivative of “y” we multiply by }\sf \frac{dy}{dx}. \\ \\ \small \sf 4\cdot \dfrac{\sec y \tan y + \sec^2 y }{\sec y + \tan y}\left(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\right) - 2 - 2\cos 2x = 0 \\ \\ \textsf{Simplifying,} \\ \\ \sf 4\cdot \dfrac{\sec y(\sec y + \tan y)}{\sec y + \tan y} \left(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\right)= 2 + 2\cos 2x \\ \\ \sf 4\sec y \dfrac{dy}{dx} = 2 + 2\cos 2x \\ \\ \textsf{Divide both sides by }\sf 4\sec y. \\ \\ \sf \dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{2 + 2\cos 2x}{4\sec y} \\ \\ \sf \dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{1 + \cos 2x}{2 \sec y} \\ \\ \textsf{We can still simplify the equation.} \\ \\ \sf \dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{1 + \cos 2x}{2}\cdot \cos y, \quad \because \dfrac{1}{\sec y} = \cos y \\ \\ \sf \textsf{By power reducing identity, }\footnotesize \dfrac{1 + \cos 2x}{2} = \cos^2 x \\ \\ \therefore \boxed{\sf \dfrac{dy}{dx} = \cos^2 x \cos y} \implies \textsf{Answer} \end{array} [/tex]

7. prove (1-cot^2x/1+cot^2x) = sin^2x - cos^2x​


We can start with the left-hand side of the equation and use the identity for cotangent:

(1 - cot^2x) / (1 + cot^2x)

= [(1 - cos^2x/sin^2x) / (1 + cos^2x/sin^2x)] (since cot^2x = cos^2x/sin^2x)

= [(sin^2x - cos^2x) / sin^2x * (1 + cos^2x/sin^2x)] (multiplying numerator and denominator by sin^2x)

= [(sin^2x - cos^2x) / (sin^2x + cos^2x)] * [(sin^2x + cos^2x) / sin^2x]

= (sin^2x - cos^2x) / sin^2x

= sin^2x / sin^2x - cos^2x / sin^2x

= 1 - cos^2x / sin^2x

= sin^2x - cos^2x

Therefore, we have shown that (1 - cot^2x) / (1 + cot^2x) = sin^2x - cos^2x.
