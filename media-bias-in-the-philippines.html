Algorithm, Social Science and Implications for Big Data, Data Analytics, Opinion Mining, Analytics as a

Abstract: Newspapers provide factual reports on current events. However, news media has been shown to be

ideologically biased, often negatively shaping the readers' point of view. News on controversial issues makes

the bias of the newspaper or its writers more visible. This study aims to measure the objectivity of newspapers

by classifying news articles from three newspaper agencies covering the 2017 Battle of Marawi in Southern

Philippines. We used Aylien Sentiment Analysis Tool to detect the bias or polarity in each news article

(whether positive, negative or neutral). Negative articles on Marawi dominated the three broadsheets (45.1%

to 59.9%) while the neutral articles were the least frequent (16.1% to 21.2%). These results indicate that

newspapers apply unequal space on the different sides of an issue, which may lead to unbalanced reporting.

We also note that despite the varying number of total articles, the three papers applied the same proportion of

positive, negative and neutral articles, which may imply collusion. The emergence of Big Data greatly

increases the speed of gathering news articles on any given issue, while the Internet of Things enables readers

and journalists to measure the objectivity of the news.

What is bias by omission?

Bias by omission – leaving one side out of an article, or a series of articles over a period of time; ignoring facts that tend to disprove liberal or conservative claims.

Bias by headline

Headlines are the must-read part of a news story because they are often printed in large and bold fonts.  Headlines can be misleading, conveying excitement when the story is not exciting, expressing approval or disapproval.  These two headlines describe the same event.

What is bias by the use of language?

Biased language includes expressions that demean or exclude people because of age, sex, race, ethnicity, social class, or physical or mental traits.”

What is bias by repetition?

Repetition bias is a cognitive bias in which there is a willingness to believe what we have been told most often and by the greatest number of different sources.

22. example of media bias ​


Answer:

Media bias refers to the tendency of news outlets to report news stories in a way that favors one perspective or ideology over others. Here is an example of media bias:

Suppose that a news outlet reports on a political rally held by a particular political party. If the news outlet provides only one-sided coverage, such as only showing positive aspects of the political party and ignoring negative aspects, this would be an example of media bias. Similarly, if the news outlet chooses to ignore certain events or information that would contradict its viewpoint, it could also be considered biased.

Another example of media bias is when news outlets use language that is designed to elicit an emotional response from viewers or readers. For instance, using words such as "terrorist" instead of "militant" or "undocumented immigrant" instead of "illegal immigrant" can evoke different emotions and biases in the audience.

It's important to note that media bias can occur in various forms, including omission, selection of sources, and framing, and can affect the perception and understanding of news events by the public. It's important for readers and viewers to critically evaluate the news they consume and to seek out multiple sources of information to form a balanced and informed opinion.>-<

Give an dialogue for positive media bias and negative media bias and give it's lesson.

Positive Media Bias:

Person 1: Did you hear about the new hospital opening in the city?

Person 2: No, what about it?

Person 1: It's amazing! The local news channel did a feature on it and they showed all the latest equipment and the positive impact it will have on the community.

Person 2: That's great. It's good to see positive news being highlighted.

Lesson: Positive media bias occurs when media outlets present a more positive perspective on events and news stories. This can create a skewed view of reality, but it also has the potential to promote optimism and hope.

Negative Media Bias:

Person 1: Did you hear about the new policy the government implemented?

Person 2: No, what's the issue with it?

Person 1: The local news channel did a report on it and they showed how it will negatively impact the economy and the people.

Person 2: That's not good. It seems like the media always focuses on the negative aspects of things.

Lesson: Negative media bias occurs when media outlets present a more negative perspective on events and news stories. This can create a skewed view of reality, but it can also draw attention to important problems and issues that need to be addressed. However, it's important to be aware of media biases and seek out multiple sources of information to get a more balanced and accurate understanding of events.


30. what is 3 media bias?​


Answer:

Media bias is the bias of journalists and news producers within the mass media in the selection of many events and stories that are reported and how they are covered. The term "media bias" implies a pervasive or widespread bias contravening the standards of journalism, rather than the perspective of an individual journalist or article. The direction and degree of media bias in various countries is widely disputed.[1]

Practical limitations to media neutrality include the inability of journalists to report all available stories and facts, and the requirement that selected facts be linked into a coherent narrative.[2] Government influence, including overt and covert censorship, biases the media in some countries, for example China, North Korea and Myanmar.[3][4] Market forces that result in a biased presentation include the ownership of the news source, concentration of media ownership, the subjective selection of staff, or the preferences of an intended audience.

There are a number of national and international watchdog groups that report on bias of the media.
