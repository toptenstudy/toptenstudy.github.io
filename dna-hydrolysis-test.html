15. define or explain hydrolysis


Answer:

From the Greek words hydro and lysis, or “water break”, hydrolyze is literally just that. Water (or H2O) breaks into two parts: a positive hydrogen, H+, and a negative hydroxide, (OH)–. These charged molecules are used to split larger molecules by means of attracting different parts of a bond. Usually hydrolysis is a chemical process in which a molecule of water is added to a substance. Sometimes this addition causes both substance and water molecule to split into two parts. In such reactions, one fragment of the target molecule (or parent molecule) gains a hydrogen ion.

18. Why does the test of acid catalyzed hydrolysis of starch work this way?


Answer:

Explanation:

The acid-catalyzed hydrolysis of starch occurs in the presence of an acid catalyst, usually hydrochloric acid (HCl). Starch is a polymer composed of glucose units that are connected by glycosidic bonds, forming long chains. The acid catalyzed hydrolysis of starch occurs when the acid catalyst adds a proton to the glycosidic bond of the starch, causing it to break down into smaller molecules.

During the process, the HCl catalyst donates a proton to the glycosidic bond, causing it to break down into two separate glucose molecules. The added proton also makes the oxygen on the glycosidic bond positively charged, which attracts a water molecule. The water molecule then attacks the positively charged oxygen, breaking the bond and forming two glucose molecules.

In simpler terms, the acid catalyst helps to break down the starch into smaller, more soluble molecules. This process is important in industries such as food and beverage manufacturing, where starch is used as a thickener and needs to be broken down into smaller molecules to achieve the desired texture and consistency.

In summary, the acid-catalyzed hydrolysis of starch works because the acid catalyst helps to break down the glycosidic bonds between glucose molecules, resulting in smaller, more soluble molecules.

Deoxyribonucleic acid, more commonly known as DNA, is a complex molecule that contains all of the information necessary to build and maintain an organism. All living things have DNA within their cells. In fact, nearly every cell in a multicellular organism possesses the full set of DNA required for that organism.

However, DNA does more than specify the structure and function of living things — it also serves as the primary unit of heredity in organisms of all types. In other words, whenever organisms reproduce, a portion of their DNA is passed along to their offspring. This transmission of all or part of an organism's DNA helps ensure a certain level of continuity from one generation to the next, while still allowing for slight changes that contribute to the diversity of life.
