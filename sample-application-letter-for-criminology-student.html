12. what are the relevance of chemistry to a criminology student?


Answer:

Forensic analysis is based on chemical instrumentation techniques.

Explanation:

Chemistry is very relevant to criminology, particularly in the field of forensics. Criminal investigation is heavily reliant on chemical laboratory and analysis. Two examples include illegal narcotic identification and DNA analysis.

Chemical instrumentation techniques, such as Fourier Transform Infrared Spectroscopy (FTIR), Gas Chromatography-Mass Spectrometry (GC-MS), and Liquid Chromatography are used to identify if an unknown substance collected for example in an illegal drug raid is actually an illegal drug and not just a harmless powder. Results from these tests are compared to a database of known results from illegal drug to confirm their identity.

DNA Analysis is also based on biochemistry techniques, where various techniques are used to extract, replicate and match a sequence of DNA found in a crime scene from one from the suspect.

Answer:

Environmental Criminology is a positivist theory that suggests crime is influenced, if not caused, by a person's spatial environment. The basis is specifically how individuals, with all their diverse attributes, become influenced to commit crimes by the elements in their immediate location.

For example

Theodore lives in an upper middle class neighborhood with its share of young families and corporate ladder climbers. On three sides are a park, high-end shopping center and a similar neighborhood. On its southern border is a economically disadvantaged area of town.

Theodore drives by a convenience store in that southern zone and buys cocaine. Using the classical method, a police sting operation arrests Theodore and some local sellers. Within days, the seller is replaced and more buyers return, unaware of Theodore's arrest. However, environmental criminologists, after studies of Theodore's crime zone, build police substations, enlist local churches and community centers to help clean up the area and offer addiction classes. Government funds help build new sidewalks and street lights. What made them think this should be the response?

SO ITS IMPORTANT THAT YOU KNOW THE ENVIRONMENTAL CRIMINOLOGY AS AN POLICE OFFICER.The Use Of Space To Fight Crime

Environmental criminologists have developed two widely used tools to fight crime based on geography. The first is crime mapping which means putting pins on maps to denote incidences of crime. This gives a visual of the frequency of crimes occurring in specific geographical locations. From there, strategies can be formulated to address the crime in that area.

The second is the Broken Windows Theory. The idea here is that an un-fixed broken window attracts more broken windows. Some studies used actual broken windows and run down buildings, while many criminologists suggest the the window is a metaphor for disorder in a specific area which is made worse if not addressed.

26. Should criminology student be apolitical


Answer:

initially,criminology examines crime from a legal point of view.This means that the criminal behavior is examined as it is in violation of the law..

Answer:

As scholars interested in political processes, outcomes, behavior, and attitudes, political scientists generally intersect with the field of criminology when they study the institutions that create and implement crime policy and criminal law, and when they explore the opinions and political behavior of citizens in relation to crime or the criminal justice system. The approaches take many forms: for example, the organization and function of the criminal justice system itself (police, courts, prisons); the political bodies that enact, implement, and interpret the criminal law (legislatures, executives, and courts); the reciprocal relationship between criminal laws and electoral systems, political parties and social movements; and public opinion. While their methods and theoretical foundations vary, political science approaches to criminology have in common a fundamental understanding of crime and justice as political outcomes that are shaped by political institutions, attitudes, and behavior. For this reason, this bibliography avoids delving too deeply into predominately sociological, legal, or anthropological works that do not focus specifically on understanding how political processes (including both government institutions and citizens) confront crime issues. Political science analysis of crime and criminal justice can be grouped into two main periods. Early work in the 1960s and 1970s focused primarily on policing, courts, and prisons as political institutions at a time when crime and violence were on the rise and racial unrest characterized much of the urban experience. The central questions of this research era had to do with public and political responses to crime rates, how criminal justice agencies coped with rising rates of criminal behavior, and the implementation of crime policy. In the 1990s, with the recognition of soaring incarceration rates and an increasingly “tough on crime” approach to policymaking—particularly in the United States and soon thereafter in other developed democracies—scholars turned their attention to the political mechanisms that generated such outcomes. This body of work focuses less on criminal justice institutions specifically and more on broader political dynamics that drive law and policy, such as partisan control of legislatures, public opinion, the presence of minority lawmakers, interest group dynamics, social movements, relationships between elite lawmakers and mass publics, and so on. This more recent scholarship is also more focused on the racial dimensions of crime and punishment, as well as the political implications and consequences of high rates of arrest and incarceration for democratic societies.
