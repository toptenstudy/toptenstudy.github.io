diazomata -a passage in the auditorium of an ancient Greek theater dividing the lower from the upper rows of seats for convenience of access

kerkides -wedge-shaped seating section in Greek seating area (theatron). Corresponds to Roman cuneus.

theatron/koilon/caves - is where the spectators sat. The theatron was usually part of hillside overlooking the orchestra, and often wrapped around a large portion of the orchestra

Prohedia - the stone seats reserved for prestigious people originally for the priests. Exodos. the final or exit scene.

Orchestra -is a large instrumental ensemble typical of classical music, which combines instruments from different families, including. bowed string instruments such as the violin, viola, cello, and double bass.

Skene -in ancient Greek theatre, a building behind the playing area that was originally a hut for the changing of masks and costumes but eventually became the background before which the drama was enacted.

22. TRIPITAKA Description Description Description: TRIKAYA Description: Description: Description:pahelp po pls​


Answer:

The Tripitaka is a compilation of Buddhist teachings that serve as the basis for Theravada Buddhism. It is the most ancient collection of Buddhist teachings. The Tipitaka is derived from the Pali terms ti and pitaka, which mean "three" and "baskets," respectively.

The Tripitaka is known in Theravada Buddhism as buddhavacana, or Buddha's Word, because it contains the Buddha's and his disciples' teachings. The content was gathered and organized shortly after the Buddha's death in the fourth century B.C.E. at the First Buddhist Council, and passed down orally until written down in the third century B.C.E.

Trikaya in Mahāyāna Buddhism, the concept of the three bodies, or modes of being, of the Buddha: the dharmakaya, the unmanifested mode, and the supreme state of absolute knowledge; the sambhogakaya, the heavenly mode; and the nirmanakaya.

According to Mahayana Buddhism's Trikaya concept, a Buddha might emerge in three different ways. This enables a Buddha to be one with the absolute while simultaneously appearing in the relative world to help suffering beings.
