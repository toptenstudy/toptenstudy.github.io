10. what is the moral lesson of the legend of the virgin’s jewel


Answer:

The moral lesson of the legend of the Virgin’s Jewel is that true love is worth more than any material object. In the legend, a young man is given a box containing a beautiful pearl—a gift from the Virgin Mary. He decides to keep the pearl, but his father is convinced it is too valuable to keep. When the father tries to sell the pearl, the Virgin Mary appears and tells him that the pearl is a symbol of her love, and that it is priceless. She then gives the young man a seed, telling him to plant it as a sign of his love for her. The young man does so, and when he comes back, he finds a magnificent flower growing in its place, more beautiful than the pearl. This teaches us that true love is worth more than any material object, and that it should be treasured and nurtured.

14. What is the moral lesson in the Legend of Abaca? And why? ​


Answer:

The Story of Abaca is a human story told through the experiences of farmers, traders, and entrepreneurs who cultivate, market, manufacture, and promote the Philippine abaca industry. Often called manila hemp, abaca is indigenous to the Philippines and its commercial production has always been centered here. The king of Spain, the book reveals, rejected abaca rigging for his sailing fleet in the 17th century. Had he not been so shortsighted, he might have found the riches he sought in his Atlantic colony, not in spices or gold, but in the strength and durability of the fibers extracted from the abaca plant that grew so abundantly in the archipelago. Some two centuries later, other naval powers, notably the U.S. and the U.K., did discover these extraordinary characteristics from their marine cordage. The author chronicles the subsequent international competition, which continues today not so much for ropes, but for the specialty papers for which abaca is uniquely well suited.How can this fiber be so strong as to hold a ship in its mooring or tea leaves in a teabag? The story of the Abaca takes you to old ropewalks and harbors in London and Salem, to mills of modern pulpers and papermakers, and to research laboratories in the Philippines.

19. Moral lesson of legend of mayon volcano


The moral lesson of the Legend of Mayon Volcano is indicated below.

But first, let us clearly define the meaning of moral lesson, which is properly known as “moral”. Moral is the lesson of a story. It is supposed to teach a reader, viewer, or listener on how to become a better person after finishing a story.

Regarding the Legend of the Mayon Volcano, the moral that can be captured from the story is to follow rules, respect authorities and respect others’ feelings.

- Pangaronon (the proud one) went to Makusog (the strong one) who is the father of his loved one named Daragang Magayon (the lovely one). Makusog is also the head of a tribe. Pangaronon went to see Makusog to ask permission to marry Daragang Magayon. However, even if Makusog wanted to allow his daughter to marry Pangaronon, he followed the tribal law of forbidding marriage outside of a clan.

- Pangaronon respected Makusog because he is the father of his loved one and the head of a tribe.

- Even if what they wanted did not work out, Makusog’s and Pangaronon’s obedience to rules and authorities should have led to a peaceful community.

- However, Patuga (the eruptive one), who was the most ardent suitor of Daragang Magayon, did not respect authorities and kidnapped Makusog so that Daragang Magayon would be forced to marry him.

- This disobedience of Patuga led to his death, the death of Pangaronon and the death of Daragang Magayon.

- If only Patuga respected authorities and respected the feelings of Daragang Magayon just as how Makusog and Pangaronon showed obedience and respect, Patuga’s actions would not have resulted to the deaths of the characters.

- This tells us to respect laws, authorities, and other people so that we can maintain a harmonious relationship to other people and to the entire community.

That is the Legend of Mayon Volcano’s moral lesson, also known as Daragang Magayon moral lesson. It can also be considered as a reflection about the Legend of Mayon Volcano.

23. WHAt is the moral lesson of the legend of ancient Vietnam


The moral lesson of The Legend of Ancient Vietnam is that distance between loved ones must never be a reason for the love, affection, responsibility and respect to vanish. Instead, it must serve as the strongest bond that unites hearts that are far apart.

This story which is mainly characterized by Lac Long Quan and Au Co is highly important to the Vietnamese because it symbolizes gender equality and cultural strength.  Lac Long Quan represents the importance of aquatic resources while Au Co, the heroine/fairy, symbolizes the significance of the highlands/forests.

24. what is the moral lesson in the legend of the firefly


The Philippines have seen changes in their folklore and myth. During the early years of the 20th century, many writers began taking the traditional folklore characters and turning them into fairy tales which would be more known in the Philippine literature as "legends." These legends are very known in the Philippines. This have been part of the filipino culture as they are stories being told to the kids because they teach moral lessons.

The "Legend of the Firefly" is a heartbreaking legend came from Bataan, Philippines.

The legend goes like this, a long time ago, there was a handsome, well-built young man who is dreaming of marrying the most beautiful maiden he could lay his eyes on. He was boastful and arrogant. One day, he decided to go up to the mountain until he saw a beautiful and lovely maiden wearing the whitest dress. For him, she was the most beautiful maiden he ever saw. As he was getting close to her, the maiden suddenly disappeared. He went looking for her in the whole forest until he was tired and mad. And so he screamed: "You are not really beautiful! Did you hear me? You have a flat nose, you're a cross-eyed and you have the biggest ears I've ever seen." He, then, lied down under a tree and saw the beautiful maiden. He was so shocked, he knew that all the things he had said was just out of madness and are not true.

The maiden felt so insulted and hurt at the same time, and so she cursed the man to become a bug. He may only turn back into a human form when he has shown the maiden that will surpass the beauty of fairy. He flew near and far to find the maiden prettier than the fairy. Day and night, he didn't stop searching. He brought a light with him to help his search during the dark night, he was the only bug with a light, desperately searching so he could go back to normal again.

The moral lesson in the story is that there are consequences in the mistakes you do. Those consequences are not revenge, but it is for us to learn our lessons and that we will know better the next time. As for the bug, he has work hard in order to get back what he wants.

In real life, we do make mistakes, we hurt people and lose their trusts. We end up doing the consequences and try hard to earn their trusts just like the firefly in the story.

Most importantly, we have to be careful on what we're gonna say. We use our mouth to speak our mind but sometimes, we go overboard and hurt them with our words. In one song it says, "Words cut deeper than a knife." Let that be a reminder so we avoid hurting other people's feelings.
