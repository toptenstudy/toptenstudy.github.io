18. The biological basis for post harvest practices is rooted in the understanding of plant physiology and the changes that occur in fruits, vegetables and other crops after they are harvest? example?​


Answer:

Yes, that's correct! The post-harvest handling of fruits, vegetables, and other crops is based on the knowledge of plant physiology and the changes that occur in these crops after they are harvested.

For example, after harvesting, respiration rate in fruits and vegetables increases, leading to an increase in the production of carbon dioxide and a decrease in the levels of oxygen. This can result in the breakdown of sugars and other organic compounds, leading to a loss of quality and a reduction in the shelf life of the produce.

To slow down this process, post-harvest practices such as cooling, controlled atmosphere storage, and the use of modified atmosphere packaging (MAP) are employed. Cooling the produce helps slow down the metabolic processes, while controlled atmosphere storage and MAP involve adjusting the levels of oxygen and carbon dioxide to slow down respiration and maintain the quality of the produce.

Other post-harvest practices, such as proper handling, washing, and sanitization, are also important in maintaining the safety and quality of the produce. By understanding the biological basis for post-harvest practices, growers and producers can ensure that the produce they produce and sell is of the highest quality and safe for consumption.

22. The biological basis for post harvest practices is rooted in the understanding of plant physiology and the changes that occur in fruits, vegetables and other crops after they are harvest? example?​


Yes, that's correct! Understanding the changes in plant physiology after harvest is a crucial aspect of post-harvest practices, as it allows us to determine the best methods for preserving the quality and freshness of the crops.

One example of the application of plant physiology in post-harvest practices is the understanding of respiration. After harvest, fruits and vegetables continue to respire, consuming oxygen and releasing carbon dioxide, which can contribute to the degradation of the produce. To slow down respiration and extend the shelf life of the crops, post-harvest techniques such as controlled atmosphere storage and modified atmosphere packaging can be used to regulate the levels of oxygen and carbon dioxide.

Another example is the role of ethylene in fruit ripening. Ethylene is a naturally occurring plant hormone that stimulates the ripening process in many fruits. To slow down or control the ripening process and maintain the freshness of the produce, post-harvest treatments such as the use of low-ethylene storage environments or the application of ethylene-inhibiting substances can be used.

Overall, the application of plant physiology in post-harvest practices allows us to develop effective strategies for preserving the quality and freshness of crops, extending their shelf life, and ensuring their safe and nutritious consumption.

28. What is the physiological basis for classifying plants as sun loving or shade requiring?​


Answer:

Some plants produce "sun leaves" in direct sun and "shade leaves" where the leaves are shaded by others. Shade leaves are typically larger in area, but thinner than sun leaves. Sun leaves become thicker than shade leaves because they develop longer palisade cells or an additional layer of palisade cells.
