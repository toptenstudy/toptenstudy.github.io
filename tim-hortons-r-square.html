4. Nathi, Paul, and Tim worked in Mr. Setati's garden. Nathi worked for 5 hours, Paul for 4 hours and Tim for 3 hours. Mr. Setati gave the boys a total of R600 for their work. How should they divide the R 600 among the three of them?​


Answer:

To divide the R600 among the three boys, we need to find the total number of hours worked and then divide the total amount of money by the total number of hours worked to find the hourly rate. Each boy will then receive their hourly rate multiplied by the number of hours they worked.

Total hours worked = 5 + 4 + 3 = 12

Hourly rate = R600 ÷ 12 hours = R50 per hour

Nathi's pay = 5 hours x R50 per hour = R250

Paul's pay = 4 hours x R50 per hour = R200

Tim's pay = 3 hours x R50 per hour = R150

Therefore, Nathi should receive R250, Paul should receive R200, and Tim should receive R150

12. 3. What are the hortones of the female reproductive system? How do they work?​


Answer:

Two hormones, follicle stimulating hormone (FSH) and luteinizing hormone (LH), are released from the brain and travel in the blood to the ovaries. The hormones stimulate the growth of about 15 to 20 eggs in the ovaries, each in its own "shell," called a follicle

Answer:

The main function of the female reproductive system is to produce eggs (ova) to be fertilised, and to provide the space and conditions to allow a baby to develop. In order for this to happen, the female reproductive system also has the structures necessary to allow sperm from a man to meet the ova of a woman.

20. explain the "looking glass self" as proposed by Charles Horton Cooley


Answer:

- The looking-glass self is a social psychological concept, created by Charles Horton Cooley in 1902, stating that a person's self grows out of society's interpersonal interactions and the perceptions of others. People shape themselves based on what other people perceive and confirm other people's opinion on themselves.

28. Horton's eleven stages of information literacy


Answer:

the Eleven Stages of the Information Literacy Life Cycle

Stage One: Realize that a need or problem exists that requires information its satisfactory resolution.

Stage Two: Know how to accurately identify and define the information needed to meet the need, solve the problem, or make the decision

Stage Three: Know how to determine whether the needed information exists or not, and if it does not, know how to create, or cause to be created the unavailable information

(also referred to as “creating new knowledge”).

Stage Four: Know how to find the needed information if you have determined that it does, indeed, exist.

Stage Five: Know how to create, or cause to be created, unavailable information that you need; sometimes called “creating new knowledge.”  

Stage Six: Know how to fully understand found information, or know where to go for help if needed to understand it.

Stage Seven: Know how to organize, analyze, interpret and evaluate information, including source reliability.

Stage Eight: Know how to communicate and present the information to others in appropriate and usable formats and mediums.

Stage Nine: Know how to utilize the information to solve a problem, make a decision or meet a need.

Stage Ten: Know how to preserve, store, reuse, record and archive information for future use.

Stage Eleven: Know how to dispose of information no longer needed, and safeguard information that should be protected.
