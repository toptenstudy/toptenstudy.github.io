2. Jane wants to solve a system of equations through elimination by combining any two equations. The number of equations she has is equal to the number of variables. She realizes that she has 10 possible ways to start her solution. How many equations does she have? Show your solutions.


Answer:

Jane has 5 equations.

Step-by-step explanation:

Let n be the number of equations and variables. The number of ways Jane can start her solution is equal to the number of ways she can choose 2 equations from n. This can be represented by the combination formula:

nC2 = 10

Simplifying this equation using the combination formula:

n! / (2!(n-2)!) = 10

Expanding the factorials:

(n(n-1))/2 = 10

Multiplying both sides by 2:

n^2 - n = 20

Rearranging the equation into a quadratic form:

n^2 - n - 20 = 0

Factoring the quadratic:

(n-5)(n+4) = 0

Therefore, n = 5 or n = -4. Since the number of equations and variables cannot be negative, the only valid solution is n = 5.

Therefore, Jane has 5 equations.

A woman (w) is (=) currently three times (3*) as old as her daughter (d). Four years ago (w-4) she was (=) four times (4*) as old as her daughter was then (d-4). What are their current ages (what are w and d)?

So we’ve got our two equations from the problem:

w = 3*d

w - 4 = 4*(d-4)

Now we rearrange a bit so they both just have w on one side

w = 4*d - 12

Replace that w with the right side of the first equation

3*d = 4*d - 12

Solve for d

d = 12

And plug that into either original equation to get w

w = 36

[tex]\mathsf{2[(x+3)+x]=x(x+3)}[/tex]
