5. What are the characteristics of frog's skin that enable it to function for cutaneous or skin respiration?


Answer:

Gas exchange in tadpoles occurs across the skin to some degree in all species. Tadpole skin is highly permeable, similar to that of adults. Gas exchange across the skin is prevalent in bufonids and some torrent-dwelling species that do not develop lungs until metamorphosis. Microhylids, some leptodactylids, and some pipids have reduced gills, thus increasing their reliance on cutaneous respiration.

Recent studies show that some reptiles, once thought not to exchange gases through the skin, may use cutaneous respiration for as much as 20–30% of total gas exchange. In some aquatic species, such as Acrochordus and Sternotherus, gas exchange across the skin is especially significant for carbon dioxide (Fig. 6.20). Even in terrestrial taxa such as Lacerta and Boa, measurable amounts of gas exchange occur cutaneously. A sea snake, Pelamis platurus, frequently dives and remains submerged. During these dives, oxygen uptake equals 33% of the total, and 94% of the carbon dioxide loss is through the skin. Exchange does not occur through scales but rather through the skin at the interscalar spaces.

27. what makes cutaneous respiration impossible in humans?​


Answer:

Cutaneous respiration is a form of respiration wherein gas exchange occurs in the skin of animals instead of their lungs. In large mammals, such as humans, exclusive cutaneous respiration is not possible to support life due to the following reasons:

Higher metabolic demands. As warm-blooded creatures, mammals and humans require more energy to survive compared to the cold-blooded animals and reptiles. The higher metabolic demands require more oxygen to sustain the demand.Humans have thicker skin to act as a barrier against microorganisms. The thicker covering makes it hard for gasses to pass through the skin, making gas diffusion and exchange more difficult.The presence of sweat can completely cover large portions of the skin, making the skin impermeable to the gasses, preventing cutaneous respiration.

A cutaneous pain receptor is a sensory receptor located in the skin that is responsible for detecting pain stimuli. Based on the information you provided, it can be classified as an exteroceptor because it responds to stimuli that originate from outside the body, a general somatic receptor because it is distributed throughout the body, and a nociceptor because it responds to noxious stimuli (painful stimuli).

Anatomically, a cutaneous pain receptor is typically a sensory neuron with a specialized dendrite (a branch-like extension of the neuron's cell body) that is sensitive to stimuli. When a stimulus activates the pain receptor, it sends a signal along the neuron's axon (a long, thin extension of the cell body) to the spinal cord or brain, where the sensation of pain is processed and perceived. The dendrites of pain receptors are often coated with a layer of myelin, which helps to insulate the neuron and increase the speed at which signals can be transmitted.
