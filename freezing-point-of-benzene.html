1. What is the freezing point of a solution prepared by adding 140 g trichothecin (C19H24O5) to 0.746 kg of benzene? The freezing point of pure benzene is 5.5 °C. The freezing point constant for benzene is 5.12 °C/m.​


Answer:

To solve for the freezing point of the solution, we need to first calculate the molality of the solution:

molality = moles of solute / mass of solvent in kg

To find the moles of trichothecin, we need to divide the given mass by its molar mass:

moles of trichothecin = 140 g / 296.4 g/mol = 0.472 moles

The mass of benzene in kg is 0.746 kg.

molality = 0.472 moles / 0.746 kg = 0.632 m

Now we can use the freezing point depression formula:

ΔT = Kf × m

where ΔT is the change in freezing point, Kf is the freezing point depression constant of benzene (5.12 °C/m), and m is the molality of the solution.

We can solve for ΔT:

ΔT = Kf × m = 5.12 °C/m × 0.632 m = 3.24 °C

Finally, the freezing point of the solution can be calculated as:

freezing point of solution = freezing point of pure solvent - ΔT

freezing point of solution = 5.5 °C - 3.24 °C = 2.26 °C

Therefore, the freezing point of the solution is 2.26 °C.

ΔTf = Kf x molality

where Kf is the freezing point depression constant of the solvent and molality is the concentration of solute in moles per kilogram of solvent.

In this case, the molality of the solution can be calculated as follows:

mass of benzene = 100 g

mass of solute = 65 g

mass of solvent = 100 g

molality = moles of solute / kg of solvent

The molecular weight of the solute can be calculated using the following formula:

molecular weight = mass of solute / moles of solute

To find the number of moles of solute, we can use the freezing point depression equation:

ΔTf = Kf x molality

molality = ΔTf / Kf

molality = (5.5 - 3.8) / 4.9 = 0.367

moles of solute = (mass of solute / molecular weight of solute)

molality = moles of solute / kg of solvent

0.367 = moles of solute / 0.1 kg

moles of solute = 0.0367

Substituting this value in the molecular weight equation gives:

molecular weight = mass of solute / moles of solute

molecular weight = 65 g / 0.0367 mol

molecular weight = 1771 g/mol

Therefore, the molecular weight of the compound is 1771 g/mol.

Explanation: To solve this problem, we can use the formula:

ΔTf = Kf·m

where ΔTf is the freezing point depression, Kf is the freezing point depression constant (5.12°C/m for benzene), and m is the molality of the solution (moles of solute per kg of solvent).

First, we need to calculate the molality of the solution:

molality = moles of solute / mass of solvent in kg

The mass of solvent is 82.30 g, which is 0.08230 kg. To calculate the moles of solute, we need to use the formula:

moles = mass / molar mass

Let's call the molar mass of the solute "M". Then, we can write:

moles of solute = 7.02 g / M

Putting this all together, we get:

molality = (7.02 g / M) / 0.08230 kg

molality = 0.0852 M/M

Now we can use the freezing point depression formula to solve for the molar mass of the solute:

ΔTf = Kf·m

2.70°C = 5.12°C/m · 0.0852 M/M

2.70°C = 0.437 M

M = 7.68 g/mol

Answer: Therefore, the molar mass of the solute is 7.68 g/mol.

Answer:

To find the molar mass of the unknown compound, we can use the equation:

ΔT = Kf × m

where ΔT is the change in freezing point, Kf is the freezing point depression constant of benzene (5.12 °C/m), and m is the molality of the solution.

First, we need to calculate the molality of the solution:

molality (m) = moles of solute / mass of solvent in kg

The mass of benzene in kg is 40.0 g / 1000 g = 0.0400 kg

To find the moles of solute, we can use the formula:

moles = mass / molar mass

The mass of the unknown compound is 5.16 g.

Therefore, moles of solute = 5.16 g / molar mass

Putting this all together, we have:

m = moles of solute / mass of solvent in kg

m = (5.16 g / molar mass) / 0.0400 kg

m = 129 / molar mass

Now we can use the freezing point depression equation to solve for the molar mass of the unknown compound:

ΔT = Kf × m

5.23 °C = 5.12 °C/m × (129 / molar mass)

molar mass = (5.12 °C/m × 129) / 5.23 °C

molar mass = 126.4 g/mol

Therefore, the molar mass of the unknown compound is approximately 126.4 g/mol.
