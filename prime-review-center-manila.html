7. why is it disadvantage of for trade and transport services to overcrowd in urban center like metro manila?​


Pure Answer and Explanation: Cities exist precisely because trade and transport (and governance and education and cultural services) converge and become more efficient as they concentrate in a certain area. The question, however, already begins from a negative assumption of “overcrowding” and necessarily implies that it is a “disadvantage”.

The proper question would be to ask whether it is possible to concentrate trade and transport without disadvantages to the city. And yes, it is possible. But it requires radical solutions in terms of how land is owned and used in Metro Manila.

You will notice that Metro Manila is actually a relatively flat city… perhaps 80 to 90% of structures are not more than 3 or 4 stories tall (you can go up to Antipolo and see for yourself). The population of the metropolis is so spread out that its suburbs spill into neighboring provinces, such that when that population goes to work during the day, the population of the Metro Manila area itself effectively doubles. High rise apartments and offices are the exception, and not the norm. On the other hand, more efficient cities - Singapore or Hong Kong, for instance - all tend to build up, rather than out. The differences are a result of the laws on land ownership, cultural preferences for owning land, the political economy of land owning oligarchs, and lack of effective and enforceable zoning.

The fact that the city is flat means that it is less efficient in using infrastructure. More roads, train lines, utilities etc. need to be built for less people in a given area, making it more costly to build and maintain the infrastructure (which also helps drive a feedback loop that favors cars over trains), while the low tax collection efficiency (and other governance problems) only makes things worse. Note how much fewer train lines we have compared to other Asian countries, and how the few train lines that we have are overused and yet need to operate under a heavy subsidy from the government.

So strictly speaking, the city is not really “overcrowded”. It seems overcrowded because of the poor design of land use policies (among many other socio-economic and legal problems). It can be less overcrowded while in fact increasing population density if we build up, while even freeing up space for parks and other public amenities. If we built up, trade and transport services could actually be cheaper, and more efficient to build and operate. If people didn’t live in Bulacan, Rizal or Cavite but actually lived in cheaper high rise housing within the city, and relied more on functional trains to get around, the perceived overcrowding would be much less severe.

Active Directory forests can be constructed according to several different architectural models, including:

Organizational forest model

Resource forest model

Restricted access forest model

The simplest of these models is the organizational forest model. In smaller organizations, this model establishes a single AD forest that contains all the organization's resources. Larger organizations may have a separate Active Directory forest for each department or division. Creating multiple AD forests provides an isolation boundary between departments. If collaboration is required between departments, a forest level trust can be created.

A second type of forest model is the resource forest model. In this model, user accounts are created within an organizational forest. Separate forests are created to accommodate the resources related to individual departments, divisions or projects. These resource forests do not contain user accounts aside from those required for administrative purposes. Instead, trust relationships allow users from the organizational forest to access resources in resource forests. Resource forests are a good choice for helping to isolate problems. An Active Directory problem occurring in one resource forest will not affect another resource forest because of the forest level boundaries that are in place.

A third forest design model is the restricted access forest model. The This model involves multiple forests, with no trust relationships between them. The users in one forest cannot access any of the resources in another forest. This design is used in high-security environments because it creates extremely strong isolation boundaries.

Single forest vs. multi-forest AD design

Although various forest design models allow for the creation of multiple Active Directory forests, such models are complex and expensive to implement. Typically, only the largest organizations use them.
