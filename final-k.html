3. What is the final volume of 2.00 L of helium gas at an initial temperature of 300K, and the final temperature of 500 K?​


Charles LawV1/T1 = V2/T2

where in, V1 = first volume

T1 = first temperature

V2 = second volume

T2 = second temperature

Given: V1 = 2L

T1 = 300K

T2 = 500K

Unknown: V2 = ?

Formula: V2 = V1T2 / T1

Note: To get the formula, apply cross-multiplication, division and cancellation of like terms using the original formula of Charles Law.

V1/T1 = V2/T2 ← cross-multiply

V1T2 = V2T1 ← divide both sides by T1

V1T2 / T1 = V2T1 / T1 ← cancel like terms

V1T2 / T1 = V2 ← final formula for V2

Solution: V2 = V1T2 / T1

= (2L)(500K) / 300K

= 1000L•K / 300K

= 3.33L

Note: "K" is cancelled out, so the unit for volume will remain which is "L"

Reminder: Cancellation of units is applicable when the other same unit is on the denominator

Final Answer: V2 = 3.33L

Problem:

If a gas is cooled from 323.0 K to 273.15 K and volume is kept constant,what final pressure would result if the original pressure was 0.91 atm.

Given Data:

T₁ (initial temperature) = 323 K     T₂ (final temperature)  =  273.15 K

P₁  (initial pressure) = 0.91 atm         P₂ (final pressure)  =  unknown

Solution:

We can solve the problem Gay-Lussac's formula  P₁/T₁  = P₂/T₂ and to solve for P₂ we can use P₂ = P₁T₂/T₁

P₂ = P₁T₂/T₁

    = 0.91 atm × 273.15 K / 323 K

   = 0.77 atm

Answer:

The final pressure is 0.77 atm

Gay-Lussac's law states that pressure and temperature is directly proportional when the volume and the amount of gas are constant. When pressure increases, the temperature also increases.

14. In a closed container at 1.0 atmosphere, the temperature of a sample of gas is raised from 300 K to 400 K. What will be the final pressure of the gas?


Given:

[tex]P_{1} = \text{1.0 atm}[/tex]

[tex]T_{1} = \text{300 K}[/tex]

[tex]T_{2} = \text{400 K}[/tex]

Required:

[tex]P_{2}[/tex]

Strategy:

This is a gas law problem. What gas law should we use?

Since the given quantities are pressure and temperature, we will use Gay-Lussac's law. According to this gas law, the pressure of a gas is directly proportional to its absolute temperature keeping the volume and the amount of gas constant.

Caution: The temperature must be converted to kelvin. If the given temperature is in degree Celsius, add 273 or 273.15 to convert it to kelvin.

The formula used for Gay-Lussac's law is

[tex]\boxed{\dfrac{P_{1}}{T_{1}} = \dfrac{P_{2}}{T_{2}}}[/tex]

where:

[tex]P_{1} = \text{initial pressure}[/tex]

[tex]T_{1} = \text{initial temperature}[/tex]

[tex]P_{2} = \text{final pressure}[/tex]

[tex]T_{2} = \text{final temperature}[/tex]

Solution:

Starting with the formula of Gay-Lussac's law

[tex]\dfrac{P_{1}}{T_{1}} = \dfrac{P_{2}}{T_{2}}[/tex]

Multiplying both sides of the equation by T₂ to solve for P₂

[tex]P_{2} = P_{1} \times \dfrac{T_{2}}{T_{1}}[/tex]

Substituting the given values and solving for P₂

[tex]P_{2} = \text{1.0 atm} \times \dfrac{\text{400 K}}{\text{300 K}}[/tex]

Therefore, the final pressure is

[tex]\boxed{P_{2} = \text{1.3 atm}}[/tex]

Answer:

P₂ = 1.3 atm

16. If gas is cooled from 300.0 K to 273.15 K and the volume is kept constant what final pressure would result if the original pressure was 750.0 mmHg​


SOLUTION:

Step 1: List the given values.

[tex]\begin{aligned} & P_1 = \text{750.0 mmHg} \\ & T_1 = \text{300.0 K} \\ & T_2 = \text{273.15 K} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 2: Calculate the final pressure by using Gay-Lussac's law.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \frac{P_1}{T_1} & = \frac{P_2}{T_2} \\ P_2T_1 & = P_1T_2 \\ \frac{P_2T_1}{T_1} & = \frac{P_1T_2}{T_1} \\ P_2 & = \frac{P_1T_2}{T_1} \\ & = \frac{(\text{750.0 mmHg})(\text{273.15 K})}{\text{300.0 K}} \\ & = \boxed{\text{682.9 mmHg}} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, the final pressure of the gas would be 682.9 mmHg.

20. If a gas is cooled from 433.0 K to 259.15 K and the volume is kept constant, what would be the final pressure if the original pressure was 850.0 mmHg?


We can use the combined gas law to solve this problem:

(P1/T1) = (P2/T2)

Where:

P1 = 850.0 mmHg (initial pressure)

T1 = 433.0 K (initial temperature)

T2 = 259.15 K (final temperature)

We can rearrange the equation to solve for P2 (final pressure):

P2 = (P1/T1) × T2

P2 = (850.0 mmHg / 433.0 K) × 259.15 K

P2 = 166.87 mmHg

Therefore, the final pressure would be 166.87 mmHg if the gas is cooled from 433.0 K to 259.15 K and the volume is kept constant, given the original pressure was 850.0 mmHg.

23. A rigid tank of 1 m3 contains air at 600 kPa, 400 K. By mistake, someone lets 0.5 kg flow out. If the final temperature is 375 K a. Determine is the final pressure. b. Determine the mass of the gas remained in the tank.


Formula:

Ideal gas law is given by

[tex]PV=mRT[/tex]

where P is pressure, V is volume, m is mass, R is ideal gas constant, T is temperature.

Solution:

At the initial state of the tank, we have

[tex]P_{1}V=m_{1}RT_{1}[/tex]

For air, the ideal gas constant is

[tex]R=287\text{ J/kg-K}[/tex]

The initial mass is

[tex]m_1=\dfrac{P_{1}V}{RT_{1}} \\\\m_1=\dfrac{(600\times10^3\text{ Pa})(1\text{ m}^3)}{(287\text{ J/kg-K})(400\text{ K})} \\\\m_1=5.23\text{ kg}[/tex]

After the process, the mass of the gas remained in the tank is

[tex]m_{2}=5.23\text{ kg}-0.5\text{ kg}=4.73\text{ kg}[/tex]

At the final state of the tank, we have

[tex]P_{2}V=m_{2}RT_{2}[/tex]

The final pressure is

[tex]P_{2}=\dfrac{m_2RT_2}{V}\\\\P_{2}=\dfrac{(4.73\text{ kg})(287\text{ J/kg-K})(375\text{ K})}{1\text{ m}^3}\\\\P_{2}=509\times10^3\text{ Pa}=509\text{ kPa}[/tex]

Answers:

[tex]a.)\:\boxed{m_2=4.73\text{ kg}}\\b.)\:\boxed{P_2=509\text{ kPa}}[/tex]

V1 (initial volume) = 3.4 L

According to the Law of Charles and Gay-Lussac in the study of gases:

In an isobaric transformation, ie when a particular mass under pressure maintains its constant pressure, on the other hand, as the temperature increases, the volume increases and, if the temperature decrease, the volume decreases (directly proportional to temperature and volume ). We apply the data to the isobaric transformation formula (Charles and Gay-Lussac), we will see:

Solving:

[tex]\dfrac{V_1}{T_1} =\dfrac{V_2}{T_2}[/tex]

[tex]\dfrac{3.4}{400} =\dfrac{V_2}{200}[/tex]

multiply the means by the extremes

[tex]400*V_2 = 3.4*200[/tex]
