How do I find molar mass?

The molar mass is the mass of a given chemical element or chemical compound (g) divided by the amount of substance (mol). The molar mass of a compound can be calculated by adding the standard atomic masses (in g/mol) of the constituent atoms.

However, molar mass can also be calculated by multiplying the atomic mass in amu by the molar mass constant (1 g/mol). To calculate the molar mass of a compound with multiple atoms, sum all the atomic mass of the constituent atoms.

21. The addition of nonvolatile solute results in a lowering of the vapor pressure of the solvent.I f 7.02 g of a nonionic solute is dissolved in 82.30 g of benzene, C6H6, the freezing point is 2.70oC. Find the molar mass of this solute. The freezing point of benzene is 5.51oC and Kf is 5.12oC/m.


Explanation: To solve this problem, we can use the formula:

ΔTf = Kf·m

where ΔTf is the freezing point depression, Kf is the freezing point depression constant (5.12°C/m for benzene), and m is the molality of the solution (moles of solute per kg of solvent).

First, we need to calculate the molality of the solution:

molality = moles of solute / mass of solvent in kg

The mass of solvent is 82.30 g, which is 0.08230 kg. To calculate the moles of solute, we need to use the formula:

moles = mass / molar mass

Let's call the molar mass of the solute "M". Then, we can write:

moles of solute = 7.02 g / M

Putting this all together, we get:

molality = (7.02 g / M) / 0.08230 kg

molality = 0.0852 M/M

Now we can use the freezing point depression formula to solve for the molar mass of the solute:

ΔTf = Kf·m

2.70°C = 5.12°C/m · 0.0852 M/M

2.70°C = 0.437 M

M = 7.68 g/mol

Answer: Therefore, the molar mass of the solute is 7.68 g/mol.

25. II. Task: Determine the molar mass Water (H₂O) Elements No. of Atoms Atomic Mass Total Mass Molar Mass (MM)=​


Explanation:

The molar mass of water (H2O) can be determined by adding up the atomic masses of each element present in the compound.

Water is composed of two hydrogen atoms (H) and one oxygen atom (O).

The atomic mass of hydrogen is 1.008 amu (atomic mass units).

The atomic mass of oxygen is 16.00 amu.

Therefore, the total mass of water is:

(2 x 1.008) + 16.00 = 18.016 amu

And the molar mass (MM) is equal to the total mass in amu, so the molar mass of water is 18.016 g/mol.

28. 5.0 grams of biphenyl (molar mass is 154 g/mol) is dissolved in 100.0 grams C6H6. Find: boiling point of the solutionbp benzene = 80.1 deg Ckb = 2.53 deg C kg/mol​


[tex]\tt{\huge{\red{Solution:}}}[/tex]

Step 1: Calculate the number of moles of solute (biphenyl).

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{moles of solute} & = \frac{\text{mass of solute}}{\text{molar mass of solute}} \\ & = \frac{\text{5.0 g}}{\text{154 g/mol}} \\ & = \text{0.03247 mol} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 2: Calculate the molality of the solution.

Note that 100.0 g = 0.1000 kg.

[tex]\begin{aligned} m & = \frac{\text{moles of solute}}{\text{mass of solvent}} \\ & = \frac{\text{0.03247 mol}}{\text{0.1000 kg}} \\ & = \text{0.3247 mol/kg} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 3: Calculate the boiling-point elevation.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \Delta T_{\text{b}} & = K_{\text{b}}m \\ & = (2.53^{\circ}\text{C} \cdot \text{kg/mol})(\text{0.3247 mol/kg}) \\ & = 0.82^{\circ}\text{C} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Final Step: Calculate the boiling point of the solution.

[tex]\begin{aligned} T_{\text{b}} & = T^{\circ}_{\text{b}} + \Delta T_{\text{b}} \\ & = 80.1^{\circ}\text{C} + 0.82^{\circ}\text{C} \\ & = \boxed{80.92^{\circ}\text{C}} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, the boiling point of the solution is 80.92°C.

30. 9.The addition of nonvolatile solute results in a lowering of the vapor pressure of the solvent.If 7.02 g of a nonionic solute is dissolved in 82.30 g of benzene, C6H6, the freezing point is 2.70oC. Find the molar mass of this solute. The freezing point of benzene is 5.51oC and Kf is 5.12oC/m.


Explanation: To find the molar mass of the solute, we need to use the freezing point depression equation:

ΔTf = Kf × m

where ΔTf is the freezing point depression, Kf is the freezing point depression constant for benzene, and m is the molality of the solution.

We can calculate the molality (m) of the solution using the formula:

m = moles of solute / mass of solvent (in kg)

First, we need to convert the mass of benzene to kg:

82.30 g = 0.08230 kg

To calculate the moles of solute, we need to use the formula:

moles = mass / molar mass

Let's assume the molar mass of the solute is M.

7.02 g / M = moles of solute

Now we can calculate the molality of the solution:

m = (moles of solute / mass of solvent in kg)

m = (7.02 g / M) / 0.08230 kg

m = 0.0852 (7.02 g / M)

Now we can use the freezing point depression equation to solve for the molar mass:

ΔTf = Kf × m

ΔTf = (5.12 oC/m) × 0.0852 (7.02 g / M)

ΔTf = 0.365 oC

The freezing point depression (ΔTf) is the difference between the freezing point of the solvent (5.51 oC) and the freezing point of the solution (2.70 oC):

ΔTf = 5.51 oC - 2.70 oC

ΔTf = 2.81 oC

Now we can set up an equation to solve for the molar mass:

0.365 oC = (5.12 oC/m) × 0.0852 (7.02 g / M)

Solving for M:

M = (5.12 oC/m) × 0.0852 (7.02 g) / 0.365 oC

M = 131.66 g/mol

Answers: Therefore, the molar mass of the solute is 131.66 g/mol.
