3. Explain the international human resource management concept​


Answer:

International Human Resource Management (IHRM) can be defined as a set of activities targeting human resource management at the international level. It strives to meet organizational objectives and achieve competitive advantage over competitors at national and international level.

Answer:

International Human Resource Management (IHRM) involves ascertaining the corporate strategy of the company and assessing the corresponding human resource needs; determining the recruitment, staffing and organizational strategy; recruiting, inducting, training and developing and motivating the personnel; putting in place the performance appraisal and compensation plans and industrial relations strategy and the effective management of all these functions from an international perspective. It also deals with how a global company can manage its human resources spread throughout the world. International businesses are required to recruit, train, motivate, nurture and retain apart from effectively utilizing their services at all levels of functioning be its corporate office, at the foreign production units plants, therefore, the process of procuring, allocating and effectively utilizing human resources in international business is called International Human Resource Management.

26. Enumerate the internal environmental factor and external environmental factor that needs to be analyze before human resource planning process is to be completed?​


Answer:

Human resource planning is an important process that involves analyzing both internal and external factors that can impact an organization's workforce. The following are the internal and external environmental factors that need to be analyzed before completing the human resource planning process:

Internal Environmental Factors:

Organizational structure - This includes the size and complexity of an organization's structure, its culture, and its management style.

Workforce demographics - This involves analyzing the age, gender, education, skills, and experience levels of an organization's current workforce.

Employee turnover - This refers to the rate at which employees leave an organization and the reasons for their departure.

Recruitment and selection - This involves analyzing an organization's ability to attract and retain qualified employees.

Employee training and development - This includes analyzing an organization's training and development programs and the extent to which they meet the needs of the workforce.

Compensation and benefits - This involves analyzing an organization's compensation and benefits programs and the extent to which they are competitive.

External Environmental Factors:

Labor market trends - This includes analyzing labor market trends, such as employment rates, wages, and skill shortages.

Economic conditions - This involves analyzing economic conditions, such as GDP growth, inflation, and unemployment rates.

Technology trends - This includes analyzing technology trends, such as automation, artificial intelligence, and remote work.

Legal and regulatory environment - This involves analyzing the impact of laws and regulations on human resource management, such as labor laws, immigration laws, and tax regulations.

Social and cultural factors - This includes analyzing social and cultural factors that can impact the workforce, such as changing demographics, diversity, and work-life balance.

Competitor analysis - This involves analyzing the human resource practices of competitors in the industry to ensure that an organization is competitive in attracting and retaining talent.
