6. We are cousins or we are both knaves. Logical Conjunction Logical Disjunction Logical Negation Logical Implication​


This statement is a Logical Disjunction.

What are Logical Connectives or Operators?

There are five Logical Connectives or Operators:

Logical Negation - a statement with a truth value that is the opposite of the truth value of the original statement, usually denoted by the use of "not"Logical Conjunction - a statement with two propositions joined by "and" or "but"Logical Disjunction - a statement with two proposition joined by "either" "or"Logical Implication - a conditional statement denoted by the use of "if" and "then"Logical Biconditional or Double Implication - a compound statement comprised of two conditional proposition and its converse

Since the statement "We are cousins or we are both knaves. " has the connective OR, then we can conclude that this is a Logical Disjunction.

10. USING LOGIC, find two newspaper articles pertaining to the Philippines and use them to answer the question "Why is the Philippines poor country"​


Everyone here is focused on the present problems (corruption, societal values) but allows people to create a more comprehensive historical picture:

Why did the Philippines become this way, and why did it not follow the path of South Korea, Japan, or China?

As many of you know, there were few differences between South Korea and the Philippines sixty years ago. A century ago, Japan was comparable. The distinction is in how governments managed and fostered (or failed to foster) the economy over the decades. My answer is based on Joe Studwell's well-researched and fascinating book "How Asia Works."

Japan, Korea, and, most recently, China has supplied lessons (in hindsight) regarding the three most critical components in creating an Asian economy:

Agriculture: The initial phase is to boost agricultural output and productivity. Instead of enormous farms owned and operated by large landlords, small farms owned and operated by individual farmers are the optimal solution. This can be accomplished by (essentially) seizing land from wealthy landlords and distributing it to impoverished farmers. This is a contentious and unattractive method, yet it was implemented in Japan and South Korea to increase agricultural yield.

        Unfortunately, no similar efforts were made in the Philippines (despite martial law or a dictatorship, which would have made their implementation much more straightforward than in a democracy). In the lack of competition, the land was held by ever-larger entities who cared nothing about output. In the meantime, the impoverished farmers were peasants who cultivated land that did not belong to them. Thus they were similarly indifferent.

The second element involves encouraging production and exports. The government's primary objective is to boost exports. A nation that sells more than it imports becomes more prosperous. A country that imports more than it exports becomes impoverished. The government encourages exports (rather than domestic markets) through subsidies and stringent regulations.

The government welcomes foreign investment, invites foreign firms to open factories, and coerces them to export instead of selling to the local populace. The government also promotes domestic export-focused enterprises to compete with their overseas counterparts. In the meantime, the farmers from step 1 have begun to become the middle class and can now buy televisions, automobiles, etc., increasing local demand for manufactured goods.

South Korea performed admirably in this regard. China is now doing this. Critical to this phase is to reject the suggestion of the IMF and World Bank (and the United States) that developing countries should open up their markets ("free market economics"), as doing so too soon would be disastrous for the developing country (before the local companies have become globally competitive). As you might expect, the Philippines enthusiastically adopted all of the IMF/free-market WorldBank's suggestions. As a result, the wealthy grew more prosperous.

Banking/Finance: The government must exert much control over banks and financial institutions (i.e., money lenders and investors) to promote exports rather than solely pursuing profits. Left to their own devices, banks seek short-term gains, such as real estate and the stock market, instead of investing in long-term projects, such as new industries (e.g., steel or aircraft), which take years or decades to generate profits.

The last thing you need at this point is for large corporations to own banks. The Philippines wholly botched this.

All people feel it is erroneous and myopic to attribute the problems to the "attitude of the people." Social ideals have little effect on the economy. Instead, the economy affects social attitudes.

Instruction and activities

1. Present students with a few of the advertisements you have gathered from around your school or local community, and begin developing a list of the different advertisements they typically encounter in their lives.

2. Ask students to work in small groups to add other types of advertising to the list. Examples may include television commercials, billboards, sporting arenas, malls, magazines, movies, and clothing.

3. Still working in their small groups, ask students to brainstorm places where there are no advertisements. After about five minutes, have students share their ideas with the class and record them on chart paper. They should find that advertising is impossible to escape and ad-free zones rarely exist.

4. Ask students what the word fallacy means. [According to Webster's dictionary, a fallacy is an error in reasoning or a flawed argument. It's an argument that does not conform to the rules of logic, but appears to be sound.]

5. Introduce a few of the fallacies that you selected from the The Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy: Fallacies website, and explore how the reasoning for each appears to be correct but is, in fact, flawed.

6. Focus on a few of the advertisements in the school or on the list students developed, and have students try to identify the type of fallacy in each ad. For example, after viewing a commercial on Channel One, ask students what technique the advertisement uses to persuade them to purchase a particular product or think or act in a certain way. Assist them in recognizing the fallacy that exists in the ad.

7. To further develop an understanding of fallacy and its purpose, engage the class in a discussion. This class discussion can be accomplished in numerous ways. Some students work better in small groups reporting back to the class, while others respond well to a teacher-lead discussion. Regardless of your approach, the purpose of the discussion is for students to become more aware of how prevalent advertising is in their environment and the persuasive influence it has on their thoughts and actions.

8. Noting how prevalent fallacies are in advertising, students should also be aware of their impact on the individual person and the larger community.

If Lincoln wins the election, then Lincoln will be President.

The Earth is not the center of the universe.We could treat these like atomic sentences, but then we would lose a great deal of important information. For example, the first sentence tells us something about the relationship between the atomic sentences “Lincoln wins the election” and “Lincoln will be President”. And the second sentence above will, one supposes, have an interesting relationship to the sentence, “The Earth is the center of the universe”. To make these relations explicit, we will have to understand what “if…then…” and “not” mean. Thus, it would be useful if our logical language was able to express these kinds of sentences in a way that made these elements explicit. Let us start with the first one.The sentence, “If Lincoln wins the election, then Lincoln will be President” contains two atomic sentences, “Lincoln wins the election” and “Lincoln will be President”.
