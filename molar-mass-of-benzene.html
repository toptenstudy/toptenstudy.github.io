5. A solution that contains 12.6 g of a nonvolatile non-dissociating solute in 400 g of benzene freezes at 3.6 °C The normal freezing point of benzene is 5.5 °C. What is the molar mass of the solute? (Kf for benzene= 4.96 °C kg/mole)​


✒️FREEZING-POINT DEPRESSION

[tex]\tt{\huge{\blue{Solution:}}}[/tex]

The problem asks to solve for the molar mass of the solute. First, we need to identify the quantities to be used. These are

m = molality

mass = mass of solute

n = number of moles of solute

MM = molar mass of solute

[tex]\text{kg}_{solv} =[/tex] kilograms of solvent

[tex]K_{\text{f}} =[/tex] molal freezing-point depression constant

[tex]\Delta T_{\text{f}} =[/tex] freezing-point depression

[tex]T{{\circ}\atop{\text{f}}} =[/tex] freezing point of the pure solvent

[tex]T_{\text{f}} =[/tex] freezing point of the solution

Second, we need to identify the given values. These are

mass = 12.6 g

[tex]\text{kg}_{solv} =[/tex] 400 g = 0.400 kg

[tex]K_{\text{f}} =[/tex] 4.96 °C • kg/mol

[tex]T{{\circ}\atop{\text{f}}} =[/tex] 5.5°C

[tex]T_{\text{f}} =[/tex] 3.6°C

Third, we need to find the freezing-point depression of the solution. The freezing-point depression is

[tex]\Delta T_{\text{f}} = T{{\circ}\atop{\text{f}}} - T_{\text{f}}[/tex]

[tex]\Delta T_{\text{f}} = 5.5^{\circ}\text{C} - 3.6^{\circ}\text{C}[/tex]

[tex]\Delta T_{\text{f}} = 1.9^{\circ}\text{C}[/tex]

Fourth, we need to find the molality of the solution. The molality is

[tex]m = \dfrac{\Delta T_{\text{f}}}{K_{\text{f}}}[/tex]

[tex]m = \dfrac{1.9^{\circ}\text{C}}{4.96^{\circ}\text{C} \: \cdot \: \text{kg/mol}}[/tex]

m = 0.38306 mol/kg

Fifth, we need to find the number of moles of solute. The number of moles of solute is

[tex]n = m(\text{kg}_{solv})[/tex]

[tex]n = (\text{0.38306 mol/kg})(\text{0.400 kg})[/tex]

n = 0.15322 mol

Finally, we can now solve for the molar mass of solute. Therefore, the molar mass of solute is

[tex]\text{MM} = \dfrac{\text{mass}}{n}[/tex]

[tex]\text{MM} = \dfrac{\text{12.6 g}}{\text{0.15322 mol}}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\text{MM = 82.2 g/mol}}[/tex]

7. Eugenol (C10H12O2) is known to be found in nutmeg, cinnamon, basil and nutmeg. What is the boiling point of a solution containing 0.144 g of this compound dissolved in 10.0 g of benzene? (Kb= 2.53 °C/m), Bpt(benzene)= 80.10 °C and Molar mass of C10H12O2= 164.2 g/mol ​


Given:

mass of solute = 0.144 g

mass of solvent = 10.0 g = 0.0100 kg

molar mass of solute = 164.2 g/mol

[tex]\text{BP}_{solvent} = \text{80.10°C}[/tex]

[tex]K_{\text{b}} = \text{2.53°C/molal}[/tex]

solute: eugenol

solvent: benzene

Required:

[tex]\text{BP}_{solution}[/tex]

Solution:

Step 1: Calculate the number of moles of solute.

[tex]\text{moles of solute} = \frac{\text{mass of solute}}{\text{molar mass of solute}}[/tex]

[tex]\text{moles of solute} = \frac{\text{0.144 g}}{\text{164.2 g/mol}}[/tex]

[tex]\text{moles of solute} = 8.76979 × 10^{-4} \: \text{mol}[/tex]

Step 2: Calculate the molality of solution.

[tex]m = \frac{\text{moles of solute}}{\text{mass of solvent}}[/tex]

[tex]m = \frac{8.76979 × 10^{-4} \: \text{mol}}{\text{0.0100 kg}}[/tex]

[tex]m = \text{0.0876979 molal}[/tex]

Step 3: Calculate the boiling point elevation.

[tex]\Delta T_{\text{b}} = mK_{\text{b}}[/tex]

[tex]\Delta T_{\text{b}} = (\text{0.0876979 molal})(\text{2.53°C/molal})[/tex]

[tex]\Delta T_{\text{b}} = \text{0.222°C}[/tex]

Step 4: Calculate the boiling point of the solution.

[tex]\text{BP}_{solution} = \text{BP}_{solvent} + \Delta T_{\text{b}}[/tex]

[tex]\text{BP}_{solution} = \text{80.10°C} + \text{0.222°C}[/tex]

11. A certain nonvolatile hydrocarbon that weighed 1.00 g was dissolved in 50.00 g of benzene. The resulting solution was boiled at 0.285 °C higher than the boiling point of pure benzene which boils at 80.1°C. Find the molar mass of the hydrocarbon. Benzene Kb = 2.65 Disregard C12H10.​


Answer:

First, we need to calculate the change in boiling point of the solution using the following equation:

ΔTb = Kb x m

where ΔTb is the change in boiling point, Kb is the molal boiling-point elevation constant, and m is the molality of the solution.

To calculate the molality, we need to know the moles of nonvolatile hydrocarbon dissolved in the solution. We can use the following equation to calculate the moles:

n = m/M

where n is the number of moles, m is the mass, and M is the molar mass.

We don't know the molar mass yet, but we can assume a value and then adjust it later if necessary. Let's assume a molar mass of 80 g/mol, which is close to the molar mass of benzene. This would give us:

n = 1.00 g / 80 g/mol = 0.0125 mol

Now we can calculate the molality:

m = n / kg solvent = 0.0125 mol / 0.05000 kg = 0.250 mol/kg

Using the given value of Kb for benzene, we can calculate the change in boiling point:

ΔTb = 2.65 x 0.250 = 0.6625 °C

Finally, we can calculate the boiling point of the solution:

Tb(solution) = 80.1 + 0.6625 = 80.7625 °C

Now we can adjust the assumed molar mass to get the correct boiling point. We can use the following equation:

ΔTb = Kb x m x (1/M)

where M is the molar mass in grams/mol.

Rearranging the equation gives:

M = Kb x m / (ΔTb x 1)

Using the values we calculated above gives:

M = 2.65 x 0.250 / (0.285 x 1) = 23.36 g/mol

Therefore, the molar mass of the hydrocarbon is approximately 23.36 g/mol.

To find the molar mass of the unknown compound, we can use the equation:

ΔT = Kf × m

where ΔT is the change in freezing point, Kf is the freezing point depression constant of benzene (5.12 °C/m), and m is the molality of the solution.

First, we need to calculate the molality of the solution:

molality (m) = moles of solute / mass of solvent in kg

The mass of benzene in kg is 40.0 g / 1000 g = 0.0400 kg

To find the moles of solute, we can use the formula:

moles = mass / molar mass

The mass of the unknown compound is 5.16 g.

Therefore, moles of solute = 5.16 g / molar mass

Putting this all together, we have:

m = moles of solute / mass of solvent in kg

m = (5.16 g / molar mass) / 0.0400 kg

m = 129 / molar mass

Now we can use the freezing point depression equation to solve for the molar mass of the unknown compound:

ΔT = Kf × m

5.23 °C = 5.12 °C/m × (129 / molar mass)

molar mass = (5.12 °C/m × 129) / 5.23 °C

molar mass = 126.4 g/mol

Therefore, the molar mass of the unknown compound is approximately 126.4 g/mol.
