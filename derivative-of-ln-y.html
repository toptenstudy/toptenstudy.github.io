1. what is the derivative of y=ln(cosh2x) :)


[tex]\large \bold {SOLUTION}[/tex]

[tex]\large\sf{y = ln( \cosh(2x) ) }[/tex]

[tex]\small\textsf{By the Chain Rule of differentiation, let u = cosh (2x)}[/tex]

[tex]\small\sf{(f[g(x)])' = f'[g(x)] \: \: • \: \: g'(x)}[/tex]

[tex]\small\sf{y' = \dfrac{d}{du} \: ln(u) \: \:• \: \: \dfrac{d}{dx} \: \cosh(2x) }[/tex]

[tex]\small\textsf{Set aside the first term and differentiate the second term}[/tex]

[tex]\small\textsf{By the Chain Rule of differentiation, let u = 2x}[/tex]

[tex]\small\sf{ \dfrac{d}{du} \: \cosh(u) \: \: • \: \: \dfrac{d}{dx} \: 2x }[/tex]

[tex]\small\sf{y' = \dfrac{d}{du} \: ln(u) \: \: • \: \: \sinh(u) \: \: • \: \: 2 }[/tex]

[tex]\small\textsf{Return u = 2x as the substitution}[/tex]

[tex]\small\sf{y' = \dfrac{d}{du} \: ln(u) \: \: • \: \: \sinh(2x) \: \: • \: \: 2 }[/tex]

[tex]\small\sf{y' = \dfrac{1}{u} \: \: • \: \: \sinh(2x) \: \: • \: \: 2 }[/tex]

[tex]\small\textsf{Return the main u-substitution}[/tex]

[tex]\small\sf{y' = \dfrac{1}{ \cosh(2x) } \: \: • \: \: \sinh(2x) \: \: • \: \: 2 }[/tex]

[tex]\small\sf{y' = \dfrac{1}{ \cosh(2x) } \: \: • \: \: 2\sinh(2x) }[/tex]

[tex]\therefore\small\sf{y' = ln( \cosh(2x) ) \implies\small\boxed{\green{\sf{ \frac{2 \sinh(2x) }{ \cosh(2x) } }}}}[/tex]

6. Find the derivative of f(x) = ln(sec(x)):


Answer:

Answer: The derivative of f(x) = ln(sec(x)) is f'(x) = sec(x) tan(x).

Explanation: We can use the chain rule and the derivative of secant to find the derivative of f(x). First, we rewrite f(x) as f(x) = ln(1/cos(x)) = -ln(cos(x)). Then, we apply the chain rule and get f'(x) = -1/cos(x) * (-sin(x)) = sec(x) tan(x).

25. Compute for the derivative of the function below for x = 2. [tex]f(x)=ln(e^{2x}+e^{-2x})[/tex]


[tex] \large \blue{\mathcal{ANSWER:}} [/tex]

[tex] \color{yellow} \boxed{\dfrac{2(e^8 - 1)}{e^8 + 1} \textsf{ or approx. } 1.999} [/tex]

[tex] \large \blue{\mathcal{SOLUTION:}} [/tex]

[tex] \begin{array}{l} f(x) = \ln(e^{2x} + e^{-2x}) \\ \\ \footnotesize \textsf{Use the formula }d(\ln u) = \dfrac{1}{u} du\:\textsf{ and }\:d(e^u) = e^u du. \\ \\ \begin{aligned} f'(x) &= \dfrac{1}{e^{2x} + e^{-2x}} \cdot \dfrac{d}{dx}(e^{2x} + e^{-2x}) \\ f'(x) &= \dfrac{2e^{2x} - 2e^{-2x}}{e^{2x} + e^{-2x}} \cdot \dfrac{e^2x}{e^2x} \\ f'(x) &= \dfrac{2e^{4x} - 2}{e^{4x} + 1} \\ \textsf{or} \\ f'(x) &= \dfrac{2(e^{4x} - 1)}{e^{4x} + 1} \end{aligned} \\ \\ \textsf{At }x=2, \\ f'(2) = \boxed{\dfrac{2(e^8 - 1)}{e^8 + 1} \textsf{ or approx. } 1.999} \end{array} [/tex]

26. ln the linear form y=3x-4, which is the dependent variable?​


Answer:

The dependent variable is y.

Step-by-step explanation:

In the equation, there are two variables namely x and y.

Variables can be dependent or independent.

Dependent variables need the other variable in order to have a value. Dependent variables cannot be changed just because you want to.

Independent variables do not need any other variables just to have a value. You can assume any value of x. You can change the value of x anytime you want.

example:

Let x=1, y = 3(1) - 4 = -1Let x=2, y = 3(2) - 4 = 2
