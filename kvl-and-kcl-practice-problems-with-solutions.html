9. What is the molarity of a solution that contains 18.7 grams of kcl in 500 ml (mw 74.5)?


SOLUTION:

Step 1: List the given values.

[tex]\begin{aligned} & mass_{\text{solute}} = \text{18.7 g} \\ & MW_{\text{solute}} = \text{74.5 g/mol} \\ & V_{\text{solution}} = \text{500 mL = 0.500 L} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 2: Calculate the number of moles of solute.

[tex]\begin{aligned} n_{\text{solute}} & = \frac{mass_{\text{solute}}}{MW_{\text{solute}}} \\ & = \frac{\text{18.7 g}}{\text{74.5 g/mol}} \\ & = \text{0.251 mol} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 3: Calculate the molarity of solution.

[tex]\begin{aligned} M & = \frac{n_{\text{solute}}}{V_{\text{solution}}} \\ & = \frac{\text{0.251 mol}}{\text{0.500 L}} \\ & = \text{0.502 mol/L} \\ & = \boxed{0.502 \: M} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, the molarity of the solution is 0.502 M.

12. Molality from weight percent: What is the molality of an 18.0% aqueous solution of KCl?


Molality is the amount of substance in moles of the solute, divided by the mass in kg of the solvent.

Now, in this case, what we need is moles KCl, divided by the mass in kg of the solvent.

We are not given the mass of the solvent, but we are given that the solution is 18% aqueous solution of KCl. This means that for every 100g water, there is 18g KCl. Since we want our solvent to be in kg, we say that there is 180g KCl for every 1000g water, or 1 kg water.

Now, we have to convert the 180g KCl into moles. Based on the periodic table, the weights are:

K: 39.10 g/mol

Cl: 35.45 g/mol

Adding these two:

KCl has 39.10 + 35.45 = 74.55 g/mol

then 180 g KCl x (1 mol KCl/ 74.55g KCl) = 2.41 mol KCl

Now we just divide 2.41 mol KCl by 1 kg H2O = 2.41 molal

23. 42.2 g of KCl is dissolved in 368g of water. Calculate the molality of a solution.


Strategy:

To calculate the molality of a solution, we need to know the number of moles of solute and the mass of the solvent in kilograms.

Solution:

The definition of molality (m) is

[tex]m = \frac{\text{moles of solute}}{\text{mass of solvent (kg)}}[/tex]

First, we find the number of moles of KCl in 42.2 g of KCl, using its molar mass as the conversion factor.

[tex]\text{moles of KCl} = \text{42.2 g KCl} \times \frac{\text{1 mol KCl}}{\text{74.55 g KCl}}[/tex]

[tex]\text{moles of KCl} = \text{0.56606 mol KCl}[/tex]

The mass of water is 368 g, or 0.368 kg. Therefore,

[tex]m = \frac{\text{0.56606 mol KCl}}{\text{0.368 kg H₂O}}[/tex]

[tex]\blue{m = 1.54 \: m}[/tex]

29. 4. If 7.4 g KCl is dissolved in water to mark500 ml solution, what is the molarity ofthe solution?​


Molarity (M)Discussion:

Molarity is one of the commonly used units in chemistry. Molarity is defined as the number of moles of solute per liter of solution --- that is,

[tex]\boxed{\text{molarity} = \frac{\text{moles of solute}}{\text{volume of solution (L)}}}[/tex]

For example, a 1.46 [tex]M[/tex] glucose solution contains 1.46 mol of the solute (glucose) in 1 L of the solution. Of course, we do not always work with solution volumes of 1 L. Thus, a 500-mL solution containing 0.730 mol of glucose also has a concentration of 1.46 [tex]M.[/tex]

[tex]\\[/tex]

Problem:

If 7.4 g KCl is dissolved in water to mark 500 mL solution, what is the molarity of the solution?

Given:

[tex]\text{mass of solute = 7.4 g}[/tex]

[tex]\text{volume of solution = 500 mL = 0.500 L}[/tex]

[tex]\text{solute = KCl}[/tex]

Required:

[tex]\text{molarity}[/tex]

Solution:

Step 1: Calculate the molar mass of solute.

molar mass of solute = (39.10 g/mol × 1) + (35.45 g/mol × 1)

molar mass of solute = 74.55 g/mol

Step 2: Calculate the number of moles of solute.

[tex]\text{moles of solute} = \frac{\text{mass of solute}}{\text{molar mass of solute}}[/tex]

[tex]\text{moles of solute} = \frac{\text{7.4 g}}{\text{74.55 g/mol}}[/tex]

[tex]\text{moles of solute = 0.099262 mol}[/tex]

Step 3: Calculate the molarity of solution.

[tex]\text{molarity} = \frac{\text{moles of solute}}{\text{volume of solution (L)}}[/tex]

[tex]\text{molarity} = \frac{\text{0.099262 mol}}{\text{0.500 L}}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\text{molarity} = 0.20 \: M}[/tex]
