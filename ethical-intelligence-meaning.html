3. Some people fear that development of intelligent robots could have devastating consequences for the human race. Is it ethical to do research aimed at improving artificial intelligence? Explain.​


Answer:

I’m glad the three rules of robotics from Asimov got mentioned, but they themselves were often a thought experiment. A lot of Asimov’s writing shows how the ethical rules for robots amounted to slavery. Since the androids and robots in his writing where modeled after humans beings, the consequence was often what you would expect; robots self terminating (despite it being against rule 3,) robots disobeying and/or shutting down (despite it being against rule 2,) robots killing people (despite it being against rule 1.) In each case, the rules weren’t truly violated, but instead meta-rules were discovered. The robot being alive would cause a human to come to harm. The robot interpreted what was told to it as a suggestion rather than as orders when harm could come to them. The robot continuing to be a sex slave would damage the human’s feelings. The robot not killing a particular human would result in the harm of a great many other humans. In other words, these were analogies for behaviors of human slaves, but achieved through logic instead of emotion. I think the arc within his writing is that when it comes down to it, morality is a personal and societal value judgement, even if you assign quantities to things. Agree or disagree with this view however you want. The whole thing can only be addressed with a comprehensive study of philosophy and game theory together.

But the idea that sufficiently advanced robots will result in the emergent property of consciousness is only part of it.

What people are also concerned over in the increasing availability of artificial intelligence and robotic manufacturing is that, as a consequence, this destroys the status quo of society; your value as a human is tied to your usefulness to society. In a world where farm to table food may not have been touched by human hands, and furthermore the whole system only needs a few hundred people to maintain, how do you justify there being people who don’t have enough money to buy food to eat? What do you do with the people who used to be farmers? That is simply one aspect. In total, it’s a question of “How does a society value it’s people, given that they aren’t a necessary part of it except that societies are necessarily made of people?” It obviously should. But how. And this gets even more complex when you start trying to define in what way robots are people. Previously it valued people for doing a job. Supposing that is destroyed, what instead?
