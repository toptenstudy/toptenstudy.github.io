4. Protein molecules partition into what phase during DNA isolation?


Answer:

During DNA isolation, protein molecules partition into the aqueous phase. The DNA is typically dissolved in an aqueous buffer, which contains salt and other components that help to keep the DNA stable and prevent it from degrading. Proteins, on the other hand, are typically insoluble in this type of buffer and will partition into the aqueous phase.

15. recommendations for DNA isolation of onion


Answer:

Sure, here are some general recommendations for DNA isolation from onion:

1. Collect fresh onion tissue: It is important to collect fresh and healthy tissue for DNA extraction. Ideally, you should collect tissue from the inner layers of the onion bulb. You can also use the leaves or roots, but the quality and quantity of DNA may vary.

2. Grind the tissue: Grind the onion tissue using a mortar and pestle or a homogenizer to break down the cell walls and release the DNA.

3. Add extraction buffer: Add a lysis buffer to the ground onion tissue to lyse the cells and release the DNA. There are various types of extraction buffers available, but a common one for plant tissues is CTAB buffer.

4. Incubate and extract: Incubate the mixture at a suitable temperature for a specific period, typically around 60-65°C for 30-60 minutes, to allow the DNA to be extracted from the cells. After incubation, centrifuge the mixture to pellet any cellular debris, and then extract the DNA from the supernatant using a suitable method such as ethanol precipitation or column-based purification.

5. Assess DNA quality and quantity: Measure the quantity and quality of the extracted DNA using a spectrophotometer, and check the integrity of the DNA by running it on an agarose gel.

These are just general recommendations, and the exact protocol may vary depending on the specific requirements of your experiment.

28. The major relationship between DNA and protein is that DNA encodes the information that is necessary to synthesize proteins. Explain how protein is made using information from DNA.


Answer:

The process by which the information in DNA is used to make a protein is called transcription and translation. Transcription involves the copying of the information in a DNA molecule into a complementary RNA molecule, known as messenger RNA (mRNA). This mRNA molecule carries the information from the DNA to the ribosome, which is the site of protein synthesis.

In the next step, translation, the ribosome uses the information carried by the mRNA to assemble the individual building blocks of a protein, called amino acids, into a specific sequence. The ribosome does this by recognizing specific sequences on the mRNA called codons, and matching each codon to its corresponding amino acid. The ribosome links the amino acids together to form a protein chain, and this chain folds into a specific three-dimensional structure determined by its amino acid sequence.

In summary, the process of making a protein starts with the transcription of DNA into mRNA, which carries the genetic information to the ribosome. The ribosome then uses this information to assemble amino acids into a protein through the process of translation. This process demonstrates the central role of DNA in the synthesis of proteins, and provides evidence for the genetic basis of life.
