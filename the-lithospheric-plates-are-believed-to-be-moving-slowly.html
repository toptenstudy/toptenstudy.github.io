The lithospheric plates are believed to be moving slowly. The convection current in mantle is the driving force that facilitates this movement.

About mantle convection

Mantle convection or mantle convection currents is the process of circulating magma currents beneath the earth when transferring heat from the core to the lithosphere so that the layers in the earth's crust move. The mantle is heated from below, cooled from above, and the overall temperature decreases over a long period of time. This mantle convection force is caused by the heat stress created by radioactive decay in the Earth's core and the heat left over from the formation of the Earth.

Convection currents from the Earth's outer core transfer hot (over millions of years) and buoyant magma throughout the mantle and lithosphere at plate boundaries and hot spots. This hot material eventually cools enough to sink back into the core. Convection currents also transfer denser and cooler material from the Earth's crust to the Earth's interior by subduction, although the radiation energy is small because of solar radiation at the surface and increasing distance from the divergent plate margins.

It is generally believed that the other terrestrial planets and the Moon share similarities in mantles.Mantle convection is characterized by the movement of the upper surface lithosphere, which is the process ultimately responsible for the formation of earthquakes, mountains, and volcanoes on Earth, Mars, and Venus.

Mechanism

Geologists debate whether mantle convection is "intact" or "layered".

• Whole mantle convection describes a long and long recycling process involving upper mantle, transition zone, lower mantle. In this model, the mantle forms convection in a single process. The subducted lithospheric plate can slowly slip into the upper mantle and fall into the transition zone due to its density and relative coldness. Over millions of years, it may sink further into the lower mantle. Convection currents can then transport the hot and buoyant material back through other layers of the mantle. Some of that material may even resurface as the lithosphere again, as it spills onto the Earth's crust through volcanic eruptions or seafloor spreading.

• Layered mantle convection describes two processes. Furs of very hot mantle material can bubble up from the lower mantle and heat a region in the transition zone before falling back down. Above the transition zone, convection can be affected by heat transferred from the lower mantle as well as discrete convection currents in the upper mantle driven by subduction and seafloor spreading. Viscosity increases by two to threefold, which causes a slowing of the hot spot velocity against the plate and an increase in the survival of chemical heterogeneity in the mantle and time-averaging with depth. Mantle feathers originating from the upper mantle may erupt through the lithosphere as hot spots.
