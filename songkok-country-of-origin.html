26. Songkok what country belong​


Songkok

The songkok, peci, or kopiah is a hat that is frequently used by Muslim men in the southern Philippines, Thailand, Singapore, Indonesia, Malaysia, and Brunei. It is often composed of black or embroidered felt, cotton, or velvet and has the form of a truncated cone. Men also wear it to formal events like weddings and funerals as well as to celebratory events like the Eid ul-Fitr and Eid al-Adha festivals. The peci is linked to the nationalist movement in Indonesia.

Future explanation

In Sumatra and the Malay Peninsula, it is referred to as "songkok." It is known as "kopiah" or "kopeah" in Java. It is also well recognized in Indonesia as "peci," despite the fact that peci has a more elliptical form and is occasionally ornamented.

According to the Hikayat Banjar, which was published about 1663 or soon after, Majapahit's elite troops (Bhayangkara) were known to utilize kopiah (also known as kupiah). Kopiah is listed as cophia in Pigafetta's Italian-Malay dictionary from 1521 (published in 1524). In Hikayat Iskandar Zulkarnain, which was originally penned before the year 1600 AD, Kupiah is described.

One Brunei newspaper article falsely claims that the introduction of Islam to the area in the 13th century led to the songkok being the standard in the Malay Archipelago. The term "songkok" first appears in writing in Syair Siti Zubaidah (1840). The songkok is a traditional Malay men's clothing linked with Islam, typically worn by local ulamas, in contrast to the traditional triangular Malay headdress of Tengkolok or destar, which is associated with traditional Malay nobility and rulers. Since the time of British administration, the Royal Malay Regiment of the Malaysian Army has worn the songkok as a component of their uniform.

Songkok has historically been connected with a headgear worn by Muslim males during ceremonial or official occasions. In contrast, Sukarno's songkok, which has secular nationalist overtones, has become the national headdress of Indonesia. Many Indonesian nationalist movement activists, like Mohammad Hatta, Sukarno, and Agus Salim, wore pecis in the early 20th century. Sukarno, who served as Indonesia's first president, popularized the peci, the country's national men's cap, and ever since, male presidents of Indonesia have worn the peci as part of their formal presidential dress. As part of their uniform, Indonesian official palace guards wore pecis as well. The team that raises the flag during the Indonesian Independence Day ritual also dons a peci, and a female peci variant with a curved back is available.

It has the shape of a truncated cone, usually made of black or embroidered felt, cotton or velvet. It is also worn by males in formal occasions such as weddings and funerals or festive occasions such as the Eid ul-Fitr and Eid al-Adha holidays.
