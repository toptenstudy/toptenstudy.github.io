14. biological data mining definition​


Answer:

Biological data mining refers to the process of extracting knowledge and insights from large and complex biological datasets. This involves the use of various data mining techniques such as statistical analysis, machine learning, and pattern recognition to identify hidden relationships and patterns within the data.

Biological data mining can be applied to a wide range of biological datasets, including genomics, proteomics, metabolomics, and clinical data. By uncovering these hidden relationships and patterns, biological data mining can help researchers better understand biological systems and disease mechanisms, identify potential drug targets, and develop personalized treatment plans for patients.

Overall, biological data mining plays an important role in advancing our understanding of biology and improving human health.


15. Identify area which data mining technology can be applied and why?


Data mining technology can be applied in a variety of areas, including customer segmentation, fraud detection, marketing analysis, and healthcare.

In customer segmentation, data mining can be used to identify patterns in customer data, such as buying habits, to understand customer segments and how to target them more effectively.

In fraud detection, data mining can be used to identify potentially fraudulent transactions based on patterns or anomalies in the data.

In marketing analysis, data mining can be used to understand customer behavior, influence customer decision-making, optimize campaigns, and identify new opportunities.

In healthcare, data mining can be used to detect and analyze patterns for better medical diagnosis and treatment, as well as improve patient outcomes.

23. What is the difference between Data Mining and Machine Learning?


Data mining is used on an existing dataset (like a data warehouse) to find patterns. Machine learning, on the other hand, is trained on a 'training' data set, which teaches the computer how to make sense of data, and then to make predictions about new data sets.
