10. why was the crime of rape amended as a crime against persons instead of crime against chastity? explain.​


Answer:

The crime of rape has historically been classified as a crime against chastity, which implies that the primary harm caused by rape is the violation of the victim's sexual purity or morality. This classification has been criticized for trivializing the harm caused by rape and for perpetuating harmful gender stereotypes that blame the victim for the crime.

In recent years, many countries have amended their laws to classify rape as a crime against persons rather than a crime against chastity. This change reflects a growing recognition that rape is a violent crime that causes physical and psychological harm to the victim, regardless of their sexual history or morality.

The classification of rape as a crime against persons has several important implications. First, it acknowledges that the harm caused by rape is not limited to the victim's sexual purity or morality, but extends to their physical and mental well-being. This can help to reduce the stigma associated with being a victim of rape and encourage more victims to come forward and report the crime.

Second, classifying rape as a crime against persons emphasizes the importance of the victim's consent in sexual activity. This can help to shift the focus away from the victim's behavior and towards the perpetrator's actions, which is critical for holding perpetrators accountable for their crimes.

Finally, reclassifying rape as a crime against persons is an important step towards achieving gender equality and promoting a culture of respect for women's rights. By recognizing the harm caused by rape and taking steps to prevent and punish this crime, societies can work towards creating a safer and more equitable world for all.

20. what do you think is the crime against humanity is being committed?​


Answer:

Crimes against humanity are certain acts that are purposely committed as part of a widespread or systematic attack, directed against any civilians, in time of war or peace. The first prosecution for crimes against humanity took place at the Nuremberg trials. Initially being considered for legal use, widely in International Law, following the Armenian Genocide.

Crimes against humanity have since been prosecuted by other international courts (for example, the International Criminal Tribunal for the former Yugoslavia, the International Criminal Tribunal for Rwanda and the International Criminal Court) as well as in domestic prosecutions. The law of crimes against humanity has primarily developed through the evolution of customary international law. Crimes against humanity are not codified in an international convention, although there is currently an international effort to establish such a treaty, led by the Crimes Against Humanity Initiative.

Unlike war crimes, crimes against humanity can be committed during peace or war.[1] They are not isolated or sporadic events, but are part either of a government policy (although the perpetrators need not identify themselves with this policy) or of a wide practice of atrocities tolerated or condoned by a government or a de facto authority. War crimes, murder, massacres, dehumanization, genocide, ethnic cleansing, deportations, unethical human experimentation, extrajudicial punishments including summary executions, use of weapons of mass destruction, state terrorism or state sponsoring of terrorism, death squads, kidnappings and forced disappearances, use of child soldiers, unjust imprisonment, enslavement, torture, rape, political repression, racial discrimination, religious persecution and other human rights abuses may reach the threshold of crimes against humanity if they are part of a widespread or systematic practice.
