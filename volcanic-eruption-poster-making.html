7. A volcanic eruption preparedness plan on what to do before, during and after volcanic eruption.1.) Before the volcanic eruption2.) During the volcanic eruption3.)After The Volcanic eruption


Answer:

What to do before volcanic eruptions:

Each one should be aware of the dangers that volcanic eruptions pose to lives and be prepared to face whatever circumstances the eruption may bring.

Prepare all the necessary things to bring once evacuation is needed.

Store as much food, water, light sources and batteries that are very useful in case of emergency.

Prepare masks to cover nose and mouth for the ash falls of the volcano.

Safety of the kids first before anything else.

What to do during volcanic eruptions:

Avoid low-lying places because lava flows, and mud flows are more likely to pass here.

Seek cover in case of ash falls and rock falls and use masks to cover your nose and mouth and to avoid breathing in the ashes.

If inside the house, close all doors and windows to avoid letting in the ashes.

Always stay indoors and watch for your kids, they might be tempted to go out.

Stay in the evacuation center and wait for further instructions and do not leave until said so.

What to do after volcanic eruptions:

Go back to your house but leave your kids with someone who can take care of them while you check your house.

When in your house, clean everything and check if there’s damages incurred. Use masks when cleaning.

Wait for further announcements related to volcano activities.

Make sure that your house is still safe for all of you.

29. Infer what makes a volcanic eruption explosive or nonexplosive​


The explosiveness of a volcanic eruption depends on several factors, including the composition of the magma, the amount of dissolved gas in the magma, and the nature of the volcanic vent.

Explosive volcanic eruptions typically involve high-silica magma, which is thicker and more viscous than low-silica magma. This type of magma can trap large amounts of gas bubbles, which become pressurized as the magma rises towards the surface. When the pressure becomes too great, the gas bubbles can rapidly expand and rupture the magma, causing an explosive eruption.

In contrast, nonexplosive volcanic eruptions typically involve low-silica magma, which is more fluid and less viscous than high-silica magma. This type of magma can release gas bubbles more easily, allowing them to escape without causing explosive eruptions. Nonexplosive eruptions can still be dangerous, as they can release large amounts of lava and ash, but they are generally less catastrophic than explosive eruptions.

The size and shape of the volcanic vent can also play a role in determining the explosiveness of an eruption. Narrow, constricted vents can increase the pressure on the magma, making explosive eruptions more likely, while wider vents can allow gas bubbles to escape more easily, leading to nonexplosive eruptions. Additionally, the presence of water or other volatile substances in the surrounding environment can also affect the explosiveness of a volcanic eruption, as these substances can rapidly vaporize and increase the pressure on the magma.
