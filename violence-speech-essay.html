3. make a speech violence against women​


Violence against women (VAW), also known as gender-based violence[1] and sexual and gender-based violence (SGBV),[2] are violent acts primarily or exclusively committed against women or girls. Such violence is often considered a form of hate crime,[3] committed against women or girls specifically because they are female, and can take many forms.
VAW has a very long history, though the incidents and intensity of such violence have varied over time and even today vary between societies. Such violence is often seen as a mechanism for the subjugation of women, whether in society in general or in an interpersonal relationship. Such violence may arise from a sense of entitlement, superiority, misogyny or similar attitudes in the perpetrator or his violent nature, especially against women.

The UN Declaration on the Elimination of Violence Against Women states, "violence against women is a manifestation of historically unequal power relations between men and women" and "violence against women is one of the crucial social mechanisms by which women are forced into a subordinate position compared with men."[4]

Violence in schools can be prevented through approaches such as providing students with conflict resolution training, using restorative justice practices, creating a positive school culture, and fostering better communication between teachers and students. Answer:

These practices are often seen as rites of passage for new members, but they come at a great cost. Hazing and fraternity violence are dangerous, unnecessary, and unacceptable practices that must be eradicated from our campuses.

First and foremost, hazing and fraternity violence are dangerous. Every year, there are reports of students being injured, hospitalized, and even killed as a result of hazing activities. These practices often involve physical abuse, excessive drinking, and other dangerous behaviors that put students' health and safety at risk. In addition, hazing and fraternity violence can have lasting effects on the mental health and well-being of those who have been subjected to these practices. It is simply unacceptable to put students in harm's way in the name of tradition or camaraderie.

Moreover, hazing and fraternity violence are unnecessary. There are many other ways for students to build friendships, develop leadership skills, and engage in community service without resorting to these harmful practices. In fact, many fraternities and sororities have shifted their focus in recent years to promoting positive values and making a positive impact on their communities. By embracing these more positive values, fraternities and sororities can help to create a culture of respect and inclusivity on our campuses.

Finally, hazing and fraternity violence are unacceptable. These practices go against the fundamental values of higher education, which are centered on learning, growth, and the pursuit of knowledge. They create a culture of fear, intimidation, and exclusion that has no place in our institutions of higher learning. We must hold ourselves to a higher standard and reject these harmful practices once and for all.

In conclusion, hazing and fraternity violence are dangerous, unnecessary, and unacceptable practices that have no place on our college and university campuses. It is time for us to take a stand against these harmful practices and work to create a culture of respect and inclusivity that celebrates diversity and promotes positive values. Only then can we truly create a safe and supportive environment for all students to learn and grow.

Hazing and fraternity violence are two issues that continue to plague college campuses across the country.
