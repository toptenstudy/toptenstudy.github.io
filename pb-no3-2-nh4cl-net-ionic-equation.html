5. What is the molality of a 0.907 m pb(no3)2 solution? (molar mass of pb(no3)2 = 331.2 g/mol; density of the solution = 1.252 g/ml)'=


SOLUTION:

Step 1: Calculate the number of moles of solute [Pb(NO₃)₂].

Assume that the volume of solution is 1 L or 1000 mL.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{moles of solute} & = \text{(molarity)(volume of solution)} \\ & = (0.907 \: M)(\text{1 L}) \\ & = \text{(0.907 mol/L)(1 L)} \\ & = \text{0.907 mol} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 2: Calculate the mass of solute.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{mass of solute} & = \text{(moles of solute)(molar mass of solute)} \\ & = \text{(0.907 mol)(331.2 g/mol)} \\ & = \text{300.4 g} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 3: Calculate the mass of solution.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{mass of solution} & = \text{(density of solution)(volume of solution)} \\ & = \text{(1.252 g/mL)(1000 mL)} \\ & = \text{1252 g} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 4: Calculate the mass of solvent (water).

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{mass of solvent} & = \text{mass of solution} - \text{mass of solute} \\ & = \text{1252 g} - \text{300.4 g} \\ & = \text{951.6 g} \\ & = \text{0.9516 kg} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 5: Calculate the molality of the solution.

[tex]\begin{aligned} m & = \frac{\text{moles of solute}}{\text{mass of solvent}} \\ & = \frac{\text{0.907 mol}}{\text{0.9516 kg}} \\ & = \text{0.953 mol/kg} \\ & = \boxed{0.953 \: m} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, the molality of a 0.907 M Pb(NO₃)₂ solution is 0.953 m.

12. Given this equation: 2 KI + Pb(NO3)2 → PbI2 + 2 KNO3 calculate mass of PbI2 (MM = 461 g/mol) produced by reacting of 30.0 g KI (MM = 166 g/mol) with excess Pb(NO3)2


✏️STOICHIOMETRY

[tex]\sf{\large{\red{SOLUTION:}}}[/tex]

Based on the balanced chemical equation, 2 moles of KI is stoichiometrically equivalent to 1 mole of PbI₂

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{mass of} \: \text{PbI}_2 & = 30.0 \: \cancel{\text{g KI}} \times \frac{1 \: \cancel{\text{mol KI}}}{166 \: \cancel{\text{g KI}}} \times \frac{1 \: \cancel{\text{mol} \: \text{PbI}_2}}{2 \: \cancel{\text{mol KI}}} \times \frac{\text{461 g} \: \text{PbI}_2}{1 \: \cancel{\text{mol} \: \text{PbI}_2}} \\ & = \boxed{\text{41.7 g} \: \text{PbI}_2} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, 41.7 g of PbI₂ are produced by reacting 30.0 g of KI with excess Pb(NO₃)₂.

24. PbCl₂ (aq) + 2 Ag NO₂ (aq) →> Pb(NO3)₂ (aq) +32Ag Cl (s)​


This is a balanced chemical equation representing a double displacement reaction between lead(II) chloride (PbCl₂) and silver nitrate (AgNO₃) in aqueous solutions.

The reactants are PbCl₂ and AgNO₃, which are both dissolved in water (aq). The products are Pb(NO₃)₂, which is also dissolved in water, and AgCl, which is a solid (s).

The coefficients in the balanced equation indicate that one molecule of PbCl₂ reacts with two molecules of AgNO₃ to produce one molecule of Pb(NO₃)₂ and three molecules of AgCl.

This reaction can be used to selectively precipitate chloride ions (Cl⁻) from a solution, as the insoluble AgCl precipitates out of the solution. This reaction is also useful for detecting the presence of chloride ions in a sample, as the formation of AgCl produces a white precipitate.
