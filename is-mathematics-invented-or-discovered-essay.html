Is Mathematics Invented or Discovered?

One example of truth about reality that held even before mathematicians were aware of it is the fact that 1 plus 1 = 2, or that there exist an unlimited number of prime numbers. They are discoveries as a result, but they were made using methods developed by mathematicians. The square of the hypotenuse of a right-angled triangle, for instance, is equal to the sum of the squares of the other two sides according to Pythagorean theory. This is a discovery, since it holds true for all right-an­gled triangles on a flat surface. However, to prove that is true, proof must be invented. Mathematicians have devised hundreds of different methods capable of proving the theorem over the centuries. In other words, math is both discovered and invented.

This view is attributed to the German philosopher Immanuel Kant (1724–1804), but it is not really different from Euclid's viewpoint. If we decide that math is neither empirical nor analytical, we might conclude that it is therefore synthetic a priori, i.e., the knowledge that is found to be true through observation but exists independently of experience. Of course, you might also conclude that everything here is merely a semantic dispute that will never truly be settled. The artificial a priori defense.

The Platonists circumvent this problem by inventing a world of forms. Mathematics exists in its entirety in a realm completely imperceptible to humans due to the limits of our sensory perception (hence mathematics is transcendent). But since the world of forms is also the basis of our reality,  we perceive it imperfectly. I may be a nice model, but to many people, it looks like “magic thinking”. After all, why does a concept like mathematics belong in the world of shapes and my chair does not? Isn't there?
