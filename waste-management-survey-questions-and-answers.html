7. "zero waste management for questions"


Answer:

Q: What is Zero Waste?

A: Zero Waste is a simple goal with far-reaching

implications. The goal applies to the whole production

and consumption cycle – raw material extraction,

product design, production practices, how products

are sold and delivered, how consumers choose

products, and more. Pursuing Zero Waste requires

questioning the view of nature as an endless

source of raw materials and an endless dumping

ground for waste. In fact, Zero Waste advocates see

nature as the ultimate production model – a system

in which all materials are cycled back for productive

reuse and nothing is wasted.

Q: Is Zero Waste the same as 100% reuse,

recycling and composting?

A: No. While much of the focus has been at the end

of a product’s lifecycle, the lion’s share of waste

and associated environmental destruction happen

before consumers see the product on the shelf.

Seventy-one garbage cans’ worth of industrial

waste are produced for each can of discarded

products and packaging set out at curbside. This

includes waste from mining, clear-cutting timber,

oil and gas drilling, as well as from refining and

manufacturing those raw materials. Zero Waste

applies to our entire environment, our economy

and our community.

Q: Isn’t it impossible to get to Zero?

A: No system is 100% efficient. But we know that

we can get darn close. By establishing a goal of

Zero, public and private organizations can focus on

eliminating waste rather than on “managing” it

Q: Are you suggesting that we should simply

stop extracting and harvesting natural

resources?

A: Zero Waste envisions sustainable harvesting

of natural resources. While achieving Zero Waste

may not totally eliminate virgin materials production,

it would significantly reduce the demand. All

remaining natural resources would be extracted in

environmentally sustainable and careful ways, with

impacts and costs translated into the price of the

finished product.

18. question:What are the 5Rs of Waste Management​


Answer:

The 5Rs of Waste Management are:

Reduce - reducing the amount of waste generated in the first place, by reducing the consumption of products and packaging, and reusing items where possible.Reuse - reusing items instead of buying new ones, such as refilling water bottles, or using cloth bags instead of plastic.Recycle - breaking down waste into its basic components and converting it into new materials.Recovery - recovering energy or resources from waste through processes such as composting, anaerobic digestion or incineration.Responsible disposal - disposing of waste in a safe and environmentally responsible manner, such as through properly disposing of hazardous waste, and ensuring the safe management of waste during transportation and final disposal.

Conducting a campaign survey about waste management segregation within a school campus can be a useful way to gather information about the attitudes and behaviors of students and staff related to waste segregation. Here are some steps you can follow to conduct the survey and interpret the data:

Determine the purpose of the survey: Before you start the survey, it is important to define the goals and objectives of the campaign. This will help you determine what questions to ask and what type of data to gather.

Develop the survey: Once you have defined the purpose of the survey, you can start developing the survey questionnaire. Make sure to include a mix of closed-ended (e.g., multiple choice) and open-ended questions to gather both quantitative and qualitative data.

Administer the survey: There are several ways to administer a survey, including online, by phone, or in person. Choose the method that is most convenient and effective for your target audience.

Analyze the data: After you have collected the survey responses, it is time to analyze the data. Use statistical tools or software to help you interpret the results and draw conclusions.

Communicate the results: Once you have analyzed the data, you can prepare a report or presentation to share the results with stakeholders, such as school staff, students, and parents. Make sure to include recommendations for improving waste segregation based on the data you have collected.
