Answer:

It was founded by Genghis Khan and his descendants and was one of the largest empires in world history. The Golden Horde's territories included modern-day Russia, Ukraine, Kazakhstan, and parts of Central Asia and the Middle East. 20. Why was Charlemagne's empire called the "Holy Roman Empire”? ​


Answer:

It was called that because Charlemagne had reunited the majority of the Western Roman Empire under his rule -- an area containing multiple different kingdoms, especially those of the Franks and Lombards. Charlemagne wanted a supranational source of authority and legitimacy that would go beyond his previous status as King of the Franks, and be respected by all his subjects.

He also wanted to associate himself with the prestige of the Roman Empire. He portrayed himself as bringing civilisation back to western Europe after a long period of division and anarchy, and restoring the Roman Empire to its past glory was, symbolically, the best way to do that.

Accordingly, Charlemagne travelled to the city of Rome, which was part of his dominions, and had himself acclaimed and crowned as Emperor using a rite closely modelled on that currently used by the Eastern Roman Emperors in Constantinople. The Byzantine Empire was at first reluctant to give diplomatic recognition to Charlemagne's Empire as co-equal to their own, but eventually did so.

In short, Charlemagne called himself a Roman Emperor because he was crowned using imperial rites in the city of Rome by the Bishop of Rome, ruled over the majority of the Western Roman Empire, and saw himself as a successor to the legacy of Augustus and Constantine.

The description 'Holy' was added to the Empire's name about 350 years after Charlemagne's time. Emperor Frederick I Barbarossa began using it in 1157, as part of his conflict with the Papacy over influence. 'Holy' is actually something of a mistranslation, though one now fixed in popular usage: the Latin word 'sacrum' means something more like 'sacred'. Frederick wasn't making a moral claim that the Empire was good and pure and righteous, but rather that it was a religious institution as well as a secular one. The Emperors ruled by God's grace, and had (he claimed) the right to make laws governing the Church and act as its protector.

Back in Charlemagne's day, it had been clearly understood that the Emperor ruled, and the Pope was merely his most senior bishop. This mirrored the position in the Byzantine Empire where the Patriarch of Constantinople was clearly subject to the dominion of the Basileus. However, the chaos in the West following the decline of the Carolingians allowed successive Popes to claim equality and even superiority to the weak emperors who followed Charlemagne. Frederick Barbarossa calling his state the Holy Empire was one shot in an (ultimately unsuccessful) fight-back.
