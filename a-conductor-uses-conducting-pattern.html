1. A conductor uses conducting pattern​


Answer:

The simplest answer is that it is the pattern or shape of the baton strokes that a conductor employs to show the time signature/meter of a section of music.

For example, using the right hand as the baton hand, in 2/4 the stroke would be: always starting at the high point, about eye level, the first stroke is always down to about chest level; that is one or the downstroke; the next stroke is back up to the starting point.

All patterns start at eye level moving to the downstroke and all time signatures eventually end up back at the starting point … that will take you back to do” (skip that last remark, just a poor musical pun, even I groaned).

So, continuing, in 3/4 or waltz meter, one is down, two moves slightly horizontal to the right (outside the frame of the torso), and three is back to the starting point.

4/4: one is down, two is in to the left horizontally, and three is right horizontally to the outside of the torso, then four is back to the top.

6/8 can either be in two strokes – down up as in 2/4 but with 3 pulses on each stroke (not done physically), or a slow 6/8 would be: down, left, left (with a bounce in the stroke), center, right, right, up.
