Definition of Crossbreeding

Crossbreeding is breeding between two individuals of different varieties within the same species. This crossbreeding technique is carried out by considering the best characteristics of the individuals that want to be crossed. The purpose of crossbreeding is to obtain new varieties of species. From the question above we know that is an example of a monohybrid. Monohybrid is related to Mendel's First Law. Mendel's first law is also known as the law of free segregation.

Gregor Johann Mendel is the father of the modern science of genetics. Mendel found that the inheritance of traits from parents to their generation has a certain pattern. So that is why we call it Mendel's Law.

Monohybrid

Monohybrid is taken from the word 'mono' which means one or single, and 'hybrid' which means to blend. A monohybrid is breeding with one different characteristic. For example, a cross-breeding between two species of cow with different color traits. One cow is red and the other is white. Because the crossed cows have only one different character which means their color is different. So, it is called a monohybrid.

The law of segregation states that allele pairs are separated independently during meiotic division in the formation of gametes. Mendel's Law I or the Law of Segregation applies to monohybrid crosses or crosses with one different trait. Gregor Johann Mendel was a scientist in genetics. He is also called the father of modern. He is the founder of Segregation Law. Mendel's first law relates to these things:

Genes have alternative forms that can regulate variations in the traits of the offspring. In this case, the concept of two types of alleles is obtained, as in the example problem, 'r' becomes a recessive allele (describes a trait that is not always visible from the outside, denoted by a lowercase letter) and 'R' becomes a dominant allele (describes a trait that is visible from the outside). capitalized)Each individual carries a pair of genes, one from the male parent (eg rr) and one from the female parent (eg RR).If the mated parents have two different traits, or they are two different alleles, then the dominant allele will always be expressed or will appear visually from the outside. A recessive allele that is not always expressed, but will still be passed on to gametes. However, the combination of these two different alleles can give new traits to the offspring
