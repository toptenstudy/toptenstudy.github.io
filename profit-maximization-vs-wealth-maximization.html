What is the Difference Between Profit Maximization and Wealth Maximization?

The key distinction between profit maximization and wealth maximization is that the emphasis of profit is on short-term income, while the focus of wealth is to increase the company over time's total worth. As noted below, these variations are important.:

Planning duration. The immediate rise in earnings is paramount under profit maximization such that management can choose not to pay for discretionary charges, including advertisement, testing and upkeep. Management still pays for these discretionary expenses under asset maximization.

Risk management. Management reduced spending under benefit optimization, which reduces the chances of paying for covers which can reduce the risk profile of an enterprise. An affluent corporation will work to mitigate damage, thus reducing the risk of failure.

Pricing strategy. In order to optimize earnings, management sells goods as high as possible to raise margins. In order to gain market share over the long run, a richly based business might do the opposite, choosing to lower costs.

Capacity planning. A profit-driven company would expend just enough of its production potential on the current level of sales and perhaps its short-term market outlook. In order to fulfill its longer-term revenue projections, a proselytized company would invest more on capacity.

From the previous discussion, it should be clear that benefit maximization is an entirely short-sighted strategy for operating a company that could be detrimental over the long term. Maximizing wealth is a long-term priority that involves higher spending and smaller earnings, but which raises the value of industry on a long-term basis.

26. Why shareholder wealth maximization should by the overridng objective of the management?


Answer:••••••••••••••••••••••••••••••••••

The shareholder wealth maximization goal states that management should seek to maximize the present value of the expected future returns to the owners (that is, shareholders) of the firm. These returns can take the form of periodic dividend payments or proceeds from the sale of the common stock.Present value is defined as the value today of some future payment or stream of payments, evaluated at an appropriate discount rate. The discount rate takes into account the returns that are available from alternative investment opportunities during a specific (future) time period.

The longer it takes to receive a benefit, such as a cash dividend or price appreciation of the firm’s stock, the lower the value investors place on that benefit. In addition, the greater the risk associated with receiving a future benefit, the lower the value investors place on that benefit. Stock prices, the measure of shareholder wealth, reflect the magnitude, timing, and risk associated with future benefits expected to be received by stockholders.

Shareholder wealth is measured by the market value of the shareholders’ common stock holdings. Market value is defined as the price at which the stock trades in the market place, such as on the New York Stock Exchange. Thus, total shareholder wealth equals the number of shares outstanding times the market price per share.
