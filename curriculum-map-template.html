1. TERM PRELIM PROJECT NO. 1 TITLE THE MAKING: CURRICULUM MAP INSTRUCTION Refer to the curriculum guide and choose a particular topic in Social Studies (Araling Panlipunan) Grades 1-6. Your task is to make a curriculum map using the institutional template and format. Develop an activity that will incorporate differentiation in your instruction. Think of a plan and creative learning activities that will fit or be appropriate to the chosen topic. Below is a sample curriculum map (attached file).​


Answer:

Here's an example of a curriculum map for the topic "Philippine Festivals" for Grade 4 Social Studies:

Grade 4 Social Studies: Philippine Festivals

Unit Goals/Objectives:

Understand the significance of Philippine festivals in promoting culture and traditions.Identify and describe the different types of Philippine festivals.Recognize the importance of participation in festivals in preserving Philippine culture.

Week 1: Introduction to Philippine Festivals

Lesson 1: What are Philippine Festivals?

Students will be introduced to the concept of Philippine festivals, their significance and cultural importance.

Activities:

Brainstorming session on what students know about Philippine festivals.Discussion on the different types of Philippine festivals and their characteristics.Viewing of a video on Philippine festivals.

Lesson 2: Famous Philippine Festivals

Students will learn about the different types of Philippine festivals and their characteristics.

Activities:

Group work on researching and presenting on different Philippine festivals.Sharing of information on different Philippine festivals.

Week 2: Participating in Philippine Festivals

Lesson 1: Preparing for a Festival

Students will learn about the preparations involved in participating in a Philippine festival.

Activities:

Brainstorming session on the different preparations needed for a festival.Group work on creating a checklist of items needed for a festival.

Lesson 2: Participating in a Festival

Students will learn about the different roles and activities involved in participating in a Philippine festival.

Activities:

Simulation of a festival parade.Reflection on the experience of participating in a festival.

Week 3: Preserving Philippine Festivals

Lesson 1: The Importance of Preserving Philippine Festivals

Students will learn about the importance of preserving Philippine festivals in promoting and preserving Philippine culture and traditions.

Activities:

Group work on researching and presenting on the significance of a particular Philippine festival.Sharing of information on the significance of different Philippine festivals.

Lesson 2: Promoting Philippine Festivals

Students will learn about the different ways to promote and support Philippine festivals.

Activities:

Group work on creating a promotional material for a Philippine festival.Sharing of the promotional materials created.

Differentiation:

To incorporate differentiation in instruction, the following activities can be implemented:

For students who have difficulty with writing or oral presentations, they can create visual representations of their research or information, such as posters or infographics.

For students who excel in writing, they can write a detailed essay on the significance of a particular Philippine festival, and how it contributes to the preservation of Philippine culture.

For students who have difficulty with group work, they can work on individual projects that showcase their understanding of Philippine festivals, such as creating a scrapbook or artwork that represents their favorite festival.

Overall, this curriculum map aims to provide students with a better understanding of Philippine festivals, their significance and importance in promoting and preserving Philippine culture and traditions. Through various learning activities and differentiation strategies, students will have the opportunity to showcase their creativity and enhance their understanding of the topic.


2. Is a curriculum map a permanent document?


Is a curriculum map a permanent document

The ansser is No. This is the explanation.

Curriculum mapping is a proactive method that helps teachers understand what has been learned, how it has been learned, and how learning outcomes have been measured. The method of curriculum mapping results in a paper known as the curriculum map. Most curricula maps are visual representations consisting of a table or a matrix.

The aim of the curriculum map is to chart the relationship between each part of the curriculum. Used as an  interpretation, coordination and preparation tool, a curriculum map   helps educators to analyze the curriculum in order to search for needless redundancies, inconsistencies, misalignments, deficiencies and gaps;    recording the interaction between the essential components of the curriculum and the expected student learning outcomes;   helping to define potential for crossdisciplinary integration;   updating evaluation methods; and  recognizing what students have learned so that educators may concentrate on building on previous  information.

Keep in mind that curriculum maps are archives of training implemented — about what has been learned  during the current school year. Projection maps or guides, on the other hand, project what will be seen in the  future.

Systematic Curriculum Mapping

While it is certainly possible for a single teacher to draw up a curriculum map for the subject and grade that they teach, curriculum mapping is more successful when it comes to a system-wide process. In other words, the curriculum of the whole school system will be mapped to ensure continuity of education.  This systemic approach to curriculum mapping will require cooperation among all educators who train  students within the school.  

Improved horizontal, vertical, subject area and interdisciplinary coherence are the key advantages of  comprehensive curriculum mapping:

• Horizontal coherence: the program is horizontally consistent because it is equivalent to the program with the same class, course or grade point. For example, learning outcomes for a 10th grade algebra class at a public school in Tennessee are horizontally compatible when compared with learning outcomes for a 10th grade  algebra class at a public school in Maine.  

Vertical coherence: Instruction is vertically coherent when logically sequential. In other words, one lecture,  course, or grade prepares students for what they will learn in the next lecture, class, or grade.

Subject area coherence: Curriculum is consistent across the subject area, where students receive  equal instruction and study the same subjects across the subject area. For example, if one school has three  separate teachers who teach 9th grade biology, learning results should be equivalent in-class, regardless of the instructor.

Interdisciplinary coherence: Curriculum is cohesive in an interdisciplinary context when teachers from different subject areas (such as  math, English, science, and history) work together to develop the primary crosscurricular skills that students  need to achieve in all grades and subjects. Examples include reading, writing, and critical thinking skills.

23. What are the strengths and weaknesses of each of the different types of curriculum operating in schools? taught curriculum supported curriculum recommended curriculum written curriculum learned curriculum assessed curriculum hidden curriculum I need it now. Nonsense report​


Answer:

Taught curriculum refers to the curriculum that is actively taught to students by teachers. The strengths of this type of curriculum include its ability to provide a clear structure and direction for teaching and learning, as well as the ability to ensure that key knowledge and skills are covered. However, a weakness of taught curriculum could be that it may be too rigid and not allow for flexibility or student-centered learning.

Supported curriculum refers to the resources and materials that are provided to teachers to assist them in delivering the taught curriculum. The strengths of this type of curriculum include the ability to provide teachers with the necessary resources to effectively teach the curriculum. A weakness may be that it might not be up to date with the latest research, or might not align with the needs and interests of the students.

Recommended curriculum refers to curriculum that is suggested or recommended by educational authorities or organizations, but is not necessarily mandatory. The strength of this type of curriculum is that it can provide guidance and recommendations for teaching and learning, while also allowing for flexibility and local decision-making. A weakness may be that it might not be properly enforced or implemented.

Written curriculum refers to the official documents that outline the goals, objectives, and content of the curriculum. The strengths of this type of curriculum include the ability to provide a clear and measurable framework for teaching and learning, as well as the ability to ensure that key knowledge and skills are covered. A weakness may be that it might not be flexible enough to meet the needs of all students.

Learned curriculum refers to the curriculum that is actually learned by students, which may differ from the taught or written curriculum. The strength of this type of curriculum is that it can provide insight into what students are actually learning and understanding. A weakness may be that it might not align with the goals and objectives of the taught or written curriculum.

Assessed curriculum refers to the curriculum that is evaluated or measured to determine student progress and achievement. The strength of this type of curriculum is that it can provide a means of determining whether students are meeting the goals and objectives of the curriculum. A weakness may be that it might not be aligned with the goals of the taught or written curriculum or might not be fair or accurately measuring student learning.

Hidden curriculum refers to the implicit values, beliefs, and attitudes that are conveyed through the education system. The strength of this type of curriculum is that it can provide insight into the underlying values and beliefs of the education system. A weakness may be that it might be unconsciously conveying harmful or biased messages.
