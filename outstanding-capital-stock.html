6. Which of the following is NOT a capital component when calculating the weighted average cost of capital (WACC) for use in capital budgeting? a. Accounts payable. b. Common stock "raised" by reinvesting earnings. c. Common stock raised by new issues. d. Preferred stock. e. Long-term debt.


Weighted Average Capital Cost

The expense of each financial institution (debt and equity) is multiplied by the appropriate weight by market value, and the products are then added to get the total cost of capital, or WACC. The discount rate for potential future cash flows in discounted cash flow analysis is also WACC.

a. When calculating the WACC for capital budgeting, accounts payable is excluded

Accounts payable is the amount of money a business owes its suppliers, whereas accounts receivable is the amount of money owing to the business, generally by clients. When two businesses deal on credit, one of them records an entry to accounts payable while the other documents an entry to collections receivable in their books.

The calculation of WACC includes preferred stock, long-term debt, common stock "raised" through fresh issuance, and common stock "raised" through the reinvestment of earnings.

The expense of each financial institution (debt and equity) is multiplied by the appropriate weight, and the products are then added together to determine the WACC. In the formula above, E/V stands for the percentage of equity-based financing, and D/V for the percentage of debt-based financing.

13. why is that corporation in the Philippines should owned by a Filipino citizen with at least 60% if the outstanding capital stock? why could a foreign not enjoy the same privelige​


The Philippine constitution states that corporations in the Philippines should be owned by Filipino citizens with at least 60% of the outstanding capital stock in order to promote national economic development and protect the country's interests. This requirement ensures that the majority of the profits and decision-making power of the corporation remain within the country. Foreign ownership of corporations is allowed, but the ownership must not exceed 40% in order to prioritize the interests of Filipino citizens.
