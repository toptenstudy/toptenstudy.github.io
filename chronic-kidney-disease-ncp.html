2. what does Chronic kidney disease affect tou your organism?​


Chronic kidney disease (CKD) is a condition in which the kidneys are damaged and are unable to function properly. The kidneys are responsible for filtering waste products from the blood, regulating electrolyte levels, and controlling blood pressure. When the kidneys are damaged, they are unable to perform these functions effectively, which can lead to a number of health problems.

CKD can cause a number of symptoms, including:

•Fatigue

•Swelling in the legs, ankles, and feet

•Dry, itchy skin

•Loss of appetite

•Nausea and vomiting

•Trouble sleeping

•Difficulty concentrating

CKD can also lead to more serious health problems if it is not treated, such as:

•Heart disease

•High blood pressure

•Anemia

•Bone disease

•Peripheral neuropathy (nerve damage)

•Kidney failure

If you have been diagnosed with CKD, it is important to work with your healthcare team to manage the condition and prevent it from progressing to more advanced stages. This may include taking medications, making lifestyle changes, and possibly undergoing treatment such as dialysis or a kidney transplant.

6. Urinary System: A Case Study Kidney failure, also called End Stage Renal Disease, is the last stage of Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD). When the kidneys fail, hemodialysis or a kidney transplant are needed for the patient to survive. This is commonly caused by high blood pressure and Diabetes Mellitus. Patients manifest confusion, shortness of breath, decreased urine, fluid retention and swelling of legs, ankles or feet. Explain why these symptoms are present in a patient with this condition


Answer:

These symptoms are present in a patient with kidney failure because the kidneys are not functioning properly. The kidneys are responsible for filtering waste and excess fluids from the blood, regulating electrolytes, and maintaining blood pressure. When the kidneys fail, these functions are not performed properly, leading to the buildup of waste and excess fluids in the blood. This can cause confusion, shortness of breath, and fluid retention, as well as swelling of the legs, ankles, and feet. The decreased urine output is a result of the kidneys being unable to properly filter and remove waste from the body. High blood pressure and diabetes mellitus are common causes of chronic kidney disease and can ultimately lead to kidney failure.
