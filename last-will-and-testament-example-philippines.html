Okay. While this is true of your Bible, this has not always been the case, and it certainly wasn’t in Jesus’ time. Here’s what I mean. The Bible that Jesus was familiar with, what we now refer to as the Old Testament, did not end with Malachi. In fact, it wasn’t even a single volume book. Rather, it was a collection of separate scrolls that were made to be read as a unified collection, and the book designed as the concluding crown jewel was Chronicles! Your favorite books of the Bible, I’m sure.

Now I’m not saying that there was anything inherently wrong with the decision to re-order the books with a new name, “the Old Testament.” In fact, we don’t even know precisely when this change happened. Our earliest manuscripts with the order of the modern Bible are in Christian manuscripts dating to the mid-300s A.D. However, this original design shape of the Hebrew scrolls was never lost in Jewish tradition up to this day. So it’s more than likely that the re-ordering was done by Christians who were no longer familiar with the Bible in Hebrew, and had therefore lost touch with its original design shape.

19. What do you think would be Jesus’ last requests, his last will and testament before He died on the cross?


Answer:

If Jesus were here today, what might his last requests be? This is a difficult question to answer definitively, as Jesus’s wishes would no doubt be shaped by the capital-T Truth that informed and animated his life. We can, however, attempt to speculate on the basis of his teachings and actions.

The center of Jesus’s life, the core of the message he sought to impart, was the love of God which is extended to all Earthly creatures. Jesus repeatedly urged us to treat one another with dignity and respect, to “Love thy neighbor as thyself”, and to reach out to all people, whether they be ‘outsider’ or ‘in-groups.’ If Jesus had a last request, it would likely be a plea for us to embrace and embody this essential truth.

Perhaps Jesus’s last words would be a plea for us to remember that all of us, regardless of race, gender, and background, are united. We share a common bond in our humanity and our individual and collective salvation can only be attained through the love of the ‘other’ – or, in his words “Love thy neighbor as thyself”. If we ever forget this vital truth, or become lost amongst pettiness and prejudice, Jesus’s lasting reminder to us would be that we must reject all divisions, as such divisions lead only to separateness, suffering and death.

Additionally, Jesus’s last wish may have been for us to view each other’s hearts and minds, to dig deep and search the Divine within, regardless of the external barriers which may divide us. Jesus, in his infinite wisdom and mercy, likely saw that the deepest core of each of us is, at its root, time eternal and limitless in its potential.

His last wish may have been that humanity embrace its members’ cares and concerns as if they were mirror-images of our own. If Jesus had the opportunity to speak his last words, he may have use them to remind us that the love within our own hearts is the same love which exists in the hearts of others; and that all of us, no matter our so-called differences, recognize in each other the potential for greatness.

Ultimately, Jesus’s last request would most likely be for us to love and care for one another and for our planet, for it is only through such care and compassion that we may understand the True Nature of God and transcend the differences which distance us from divinity. In this way, living in accordance with Jesus’s teachings is an effort to honor his last wish, no matter how distant or forgotten. If he had the chance to speak his final words, it is likely that such words would be a reminder of the power of love and light that has touched so many over the centuries and beyond.

23. What is a “judge” according to the Old Testament? Give 5 examples ​


Answer:

In the Hebrew Bible, Moses is described as a shofet over the Israelites and appoints others to whom cases were delegated in accordance with the advice of Jethro, his Midianite father-in-law.[11] The Book of Judges mentions eleven leaders who are said to "judge" Israel: Othniel, Shamgar, Deborah, Gideon, Tola, Jair, Jephthah, Ibzan, Elon, Abdon, and Samson. Ehud, described in the text between Othniel and Shamgar, is never said to judge Israel, but is usually included as a judge because the history of his leadership follows a set pattern characteristic of five of the others.[12] The First Book of Samuel mentions Eli and Samuel, as well as Joel and Abiah (two sons of Samuel). The First Book of Chronicles mentions Kenaniah and his sons. The Second Book of Chronicles mentions Amariah and Zebadiah (son of Ishmael).

The Book of Judges also recounts the story of Abimelech, an illegitimate son of Gideon, who was appointed as a judge-like leader by the citizens of the city of Shechem. He was later overthrown during a local conflict, and the classification of Abimelech as a judge is questionable.[13]

The biblical text does not generally describe these leaders as "a judge", but says that they "judged Israel", using the verb שָׁפַט (š-f-t).[14][page needed] Thus, Othniel "judged Israel" (Judges 3:10), Tola "judged Israel twenty-three years" (Judges 10:2), and Jair judged Israel twenty-two years (Judges 10:3).
